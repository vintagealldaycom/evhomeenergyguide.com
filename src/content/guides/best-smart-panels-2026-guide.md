There is no single winner in smart panels, because the products on the market aren't variations of one design — they're genuinely different architectures aimed at different homes. Some replace your whole panel; some snap smart breakers into a load center; some bolt a smart subpanel beside what you already have. This guide walks through the real options — SPAN, Leviton, Lumin, Savant, and Eaton's smart breakers — and explains *why* each design fits some situations better than others, so you can match the architecture to your home rather than shopping by brand buzz. We haven't lab-tested these units, so you won't find rankings here — just the structural differences that actually drive the decision.

Which architecture fits you is mostly decided by two facts about your house: whether your current panel is worth keeping, and how many circuits you truly want smart. A failing or full panel points to replacement; a healthy panel with focused needs points to the add-on or breaker routes. That single framing sorts most of the market.

## SPAN: the all-in replacement

SPAN's panel is the most software-forward take: replace the box entirely and every circuit becomes individually monitored and controllable from one app. It was the first panel certified to UL 3141, the power-control-systems standard, and it targets exactly the whole-home cases this site covers — [fitting EV charging on tight service](/smart-panel-vs-main-panel-upgrade/), prioritizing circuits during [battery backup](/smart-panel-vs-transfer-switch/), and coordinating electrification. Hardware alone retails around $3,500 before installation, which is real money — the bet is that one unified brain over every circuit is worth it. In early 2026 SPAN announced a partnership and $75M investment from Eaton, one of the biggest names in electrical equipment, aimed at distribution reach and driving costs down — a meaningful signal for long-term support, which matters for a product this cloud-connected.

**Fits:** whole-home electrification, solar-plus-battery homes, and anyone whose panel needed replacing anyway. **Fights:** budgets, and homes that only ever wanted three circuits smart.

## Leviton: smart breakers in a familiar box

Leviton approaches from the electrician's side: its load center looks and installs like a conventional panel, and you add its smart breakers only to the circuits you care about — the rest stay ordinary and cheap. Contractors know the format, installs are faster, and the entry cost is much lower than a full smart panel; you can start with two smart circuits and grow. Owner reports favor pairing the hub with wired Ethernet for reliability. The trade-off mirrors the [smart breakers vs smart panel](/smart-panel-vs-smart-breakers/) analysis: per-breaker costs climb as your ambitions grow, and whole-home orchestration is looser than a single-brain panel.

**Fits:** targeted needs, staged budgets, new panels being installed anyway. **Fights:** sprawling smart-circuit wish lists, deep solar/battery orchestration.

## Lumin: keep your panel, add a smart layer

Lumin's device is a smart subpanel that mounts beside your existing panel and takes over control of a dozen or so selected circuits. Nothing about your main panel changes — which is the whole appeal. It's the retrofit-friendly path to [load management](/smart-panel-vs-load-management/) and backup prioritization when your panel is healthy and replacing it would be waste. It's also notably battery-agnostic, pairing with many storage brands.

**Fits:** healthy existing panels, battery retrofits, renters-of-their-own-panel situations where a full swap is overkill. **Fights:** homes that want every circuit smart, or whose panel is due for replacement regardless.

## Savant and Eaton: the modular and the incumbent

Savant sells smart-circuit modules that bring monitoring and control to selected circuits inside existing panel setups — conceptually like Lumin's "keep your panel" philosophy with a strong smart-home integration story. Eaton, the electrical incumbent, sells AbleEdge smart breakers for compatible panels and now backs SPAN as well — so its DNA shows up in both the breaker route and the replacement route. If your electrician already works in an Eaton-compatible ecosystem, the breaker path there deserves a look.

## The cautionary tale: Schneider Pulse

One more data point belongs in any honest overview: Schneider Electric — a giant of the industry — discontinued its Pulse smart panel. Owners of discontinued cloud-dependent hardware face uncertain app support and feature futures. The lesson isn't "avoid smart panels"; it's that vendor staying power is a real spec. Weigh company commitment (SPAN's Eaton backing, Leviton's and Eaton's scale, Lumin's installer network) the same way you weigh amps and app screenshots.

Prices move, models update, and incentives change — in the U.S., panel upgrades tied to efficiency projects may qualify for federal tax credits, which can meaningfully offset any of these routes. Verify current pricing and incentive eligibility for your specific project before deciding.

## Reading a quote like an insider

Whichever architecture you shortlist, the quotes you receive will hide the decision-relevant numbers inside lump sums, so ask for the split explicitly: hardware, labor, permit and inspection, and any service-entrance or utility work. Two quotes for "a smart panel installed" can differ by thousands simply because one includes relocating the panel or upsizing the service entrance and the other doesn't. If a full-replacement quote (SPAN-style) looks shocking next to a smart-breaker quote (Leviton-style), check whether the replacement quote quietly includes work — a panel relocation, a mast replacement — that the breaker quote assumes away and that you might have needed regardless.

Also ask each bidder two questions that reveal more than any brochure. First: "What happens to each feature if the vendor's cloud goes away?" — the answer maps which capabilities are local (protection always; often schedules) versus cloud-dependent (remote access, sometimes analytics), which is exactly the risk the Schneider Pulse story teaches. Second: "How many of these have you installed?" — smart panels reward installer familiarity, and an electrician fluent in your chosen ecosystem will configure load-management priorities correctly the first time instead of leaving defaults that never trip.

Finally, sequence the purchase around your bigger projects. Buying the smart panel *during* the solar-and-battery install, or during the EV-charger project that triggered a load calculation, lets one permit, one inspection, and one electrician visit cover work that would cost more as three separate mobilizations.

## Quick answers

**Which smart panel is objectively best?** None — the architectures serve different homes. Match replacement vs breakers vs add-on to your panel's health and your circuit count first; brands second.

**Are these DIY installs?** No. All involve panel work that belongs to a licensed electrician, with permits and inspection.

**What happened to Schneider's smart panel?** The Pulse was discontinued — a reminder to weigh vendor longevity as part of the purchase.

**Do smart panels qualify for incentives?** Panel upgrades connected to qualifying electrification or efficiency work may be eligible for U.S. federal tax credits and utility rebates; confirm current rules for your project.

## The bottom line

Shop the architecture before the brand. A due-for-replacement panel points to SPAN's all-in approach; a healthy panel with focused needs points to Leviton-style smart breakers or a Lumin-style add-on; visibility-only goals point away from panels entirely. Then weigh vendor staying power — the Schneider Pulse story is the industry telling you that support matters as much as specs.
