This is the decision that actually saves money, because the two options attack the same problem — "I want to add big loads and my service feels maxed" — from opposite directions. A **main panel upgrade** (say, 100 amps to 200 amps) buys you more raw capacity from the utility. A **smart panel** keeps your existing capacity but manages it so intelligently that you may not need more. One adds amps; the other avoids needing them. Choosing wrong means either paying for a service upgrade you didn't need, or buying a smart panel that can't overcome a genuine capacity shortfall.

The key insight is that most homes rarely use their full service capacity at once — the limit is a *peak* problem, not an *average* problem. A smart panel exploits that: it lets big loads run when the house is quiet and throttles them when demand spikes, so your true peak stays under the existing rating. A service upgrade ignores cleverness and just raises the ceiling. When your problem is occasional peaks, the smart panel dodges the upgrade; when your problem is genuinely too much total demand, only more amps will do.

Service upgrades have a nasty habit of triggering costs beyond the panel itself — a heavier service entrance, a new meter socket, utility coordination, sometimes trenching. When those apply, a smart panel that lets you skip the upgrade entirely can win on price *and* hand you metering and control as a bonus. But when the upgrade is modest and your demand is genuinely high, paying for real amps is the honest fix, and layering a smart panel on top is optional.

## Sometimes the answer is both

Heavy electrifiers occasionally need the upgrade *and* want the smart panel — more amps to cover true peak demand, plus intelligent management, metering, and backup on top. That's the priciest path but sometimes the right one for an all-electric home with multiple EVs. For most households adding their first EV or heat pump, though, the smart panel alone quietly resolves the "am I out of power?" worry without a utility project. ## A worked example: the load calculation that decides it

Here's how this decision actually resolves in practice — with arithmetic, not vibes. An electrician runs a standard load calculation on your 100-amp home: square footage, fixed appliances, HVAC, and the demand factors the code allows. Suppose it lands around 70–80 amps of calculated demand. You want to add a 48-amp continuous EV charger, which counts heavily in the calc. On paper, you're over — and the traditional conclusion is a 200-amp service upgrade.

Before signing that quote, look at what "over" means. Your *actual* peak — visible on a monitor or in utility interval data — might brush 60 amps for a few minutes at dinnertime and idle far lower overnight, exactly when the car would charge. The shortfall isn't power; it's the *paper coincidence* of every load peaking at once, which never happens in your house. This is the precise gap a smart panel (or a listed [load-management device](/smart-panel-vs-load-management/)) is built to close: it guarantees the charger yields whenever household demand climbs, so the combined peak provably stays under 100 amps, and the inspector can sign off without new service.

Now change one input. Make it two EVs, a heat-pump conversion, an induction range, and a heat-pump water heater on that same 100-amp service. The calculation doesn't just exceed the paper limit — the *real* simultaneous demand does too, and no scheduling trick can run a heat pump, a range, and two chargers on amps that don't exist. That's a genuine capacity problem, and the upgrade is the honest fix, with a smart layer optional on top.

Same house, same panel, opposite answers — the load calculation plus a look at your real peak is what tells you which case you're in. Spend the few hundred dollars on that assessment before committing thousands in either direction.

## Quick answers

**Will a smart panel let me skip a service upgrade?** Often, for one or two big loads, by keeping their peaks apart. Not if your total demand truly exceeds the service.

**Is a service upgrade ever unavoidable?** Yes — full electrification of a small service, or an unsafe/undersized panel, can require more amps that no management can conjure.

**Can I do both?** Yes — heavy all-electric homes sometimes upgrade *and* go smart. Most first-EV homes don't need to.

**How do I know which I need?** Have an electrician run a load calculation. It tells you whether your problem is peaks (manageable) or true capacity (needs amps).

**Does a service upgrade add home value?** It can help at resale in older housing stock, since buyers and inspectors flag small services — but it's capacity insurance, not a feature. A smart panel is more visible in daily life; an upgrade matters on inspection day.

**How long does each project take?** A smart panel swap is typically a day of electrician work. A service upgrade adds utility scheduling — meter pull, possible service-drop work, inspection — which can stretch the calendar to weeks even when the labor itself is short.

## The bottom line

If you're adding a load or two and your service is merely tight, a smart panel's [load management](/smart-panel-vs-load-management/) can fit them without buying amps — and often costs less than an upgrade that would trigger utility work. If you're fully electrifying a small service, or the panel is unsafe, you need the real capacity an upgrade provides. Get a load calculation first: it tells you whether your problem is timing (a smart-panel job) or total power (an upgrade job).
