A full charge from empty to full is the number people picture when they imagine the EV equivalent of filling a tank, and at home it typically costs somewhere between 8 and 20 dollars depending on the size of your battery and your electricity rate. The reason the range is wide is simple: a full charge is your battery capacity in kWh multiplied by your rate, plus about ten percent for charging losses, and batteries range from around 40 kWh in compact EVs to over 130 kWh in big trucks. This guide works real examples across pack sizes at both average and off-peak rates, then explains why the 0-to-100 figure, while satisfying, is not actually how most owners charge day to day.

## The full-charge formula

  <div class="callout info"><span class="co-icon"></span><div><strong>Full charge cost = battery kWh × your rate ÷ 0.9.</strong> The 0.9 accounts for roughly 10 percent Level 2 charging losses. A 60 kWh pack at 18.8 cents costs about 60 × 0.188 ÷ 0.9, or roughly 12.50 dollars.</div></div>

Every full-charge estimate is that one line. Your battery capacity is on the spec sheet in kWh; your rate is on your bill. Divide by 0.9 because about a tenth of the power from the wall becomes heat rather than stored energy on a Level 2 charger. That is the whole calculation, and it is exactly what the [Sim-Lab charging calculator](/en/sim-lab/charging-cost/) does when you enter your pack size and rate.

The pattern is clear: bigger battery, bigger full-charge bill, but every one of them buys a lot of miles. A 60 kWh pack at 12.50 dollars delivers roughly 200 to 240 miles in a typical EV, which pencils out to about 5 to 6 cents a mile, exactly the figure in our [cost-to-charge guide](/how-much-does-it-cost-to-charge-an-ev-at-home-2026-guide/). Off-peak charging roughly halves every one of these numbers, which is why the right-hand column matters more than the headline. In a high-rate state near 35 cents, multiply the average column by nearly two.

The full-charge number is a useful benchmark, but it overstates a typical session. Most owners plug in nightly and add only the energy they used that day, often 10 to 20 kWh, which is a few dollars, not the full-tank figure. Many also cap daily charging around 80 to 90 percent because keeping a lithium battery near full all the time accelerates aging, so a true 0-to-100 fill is reserved for the morning of a long trip. In practice your daily cost is a fraction of the full-charge table above, which is why monthly totals feel modest even for big-battery vehicles, as our [monthly guide](/how-much-it-costs-to-charge-an-ev-per-month-real-bills-explained/) shows.

## Charging losses, briefly

The reason a full charge costs slightly more than battery capacity times rate is that charging is not perfectly efficient. On a Level 2 home charger, roughly 90 percent of the energy drawn from the wall reaches the battery; the rest is lost as heat in the charger, the onboard converter, and the battery management system. Level 1 charging on a standard outlet is a bit less efficient, nearer 85 percent. This is why the honest formula divides by efficiency, and why the kWh your utility bills you for is always a little higher than the kWh your car reports adding, a nuance our [power-use guide](/how-much-does-a-level-2-charger-add-to-your-electric-bill-2026-guide/) covers.

## The road-trip exception

A full charge at home is cheap; the same full charge on the road is not. Public DC fast charging runs 30 to 50 cents per kWh, so a 60 kWh fill that costs 12.50 dollars at home can cost 20 to 30 dollars on the highway. This is the core reason experienced owners charge to full at home before departing and use fast charging only to extend a trip, keeping the expensive kWh to a minimum. For daily life, the home full-charge numbers above are your reality; fast charging is the occasional premium you pay for distance.

## Home versus on the road, one full charge

The same full charge tells two very different cost stories depending on where you plug in, and seeing them side by side clarifies why owners guard their home charging. That 60 kWh pack that costs about 12.50 dollars to fill at the average home rate, and roughly 6 dollars off-peak, costs 20 to 30 dollars filled at a public DC fast charger running 30 to 50 cents per kWh. The energy delivered is identical; only the price per kWh differs. This is the entire reason experienced owners leave home with a full battery before a trip and treat fast charging as a way to extend range rather than a primary fuel source. For the big-battery truck in the table, the gap is even starker, since a full public fast charge can approach the cost of a tank of gas. The lesson from the full-charge math is simple: fill at home where a full charge is cheap, and reserve the expensive public kWh for when distance genuinely requires it.

## Quick answers

**What does a full charge cost?** Roughly 8 to 20 dollars at home for most cars at average rates, more for big trucks, about half that off-peak.

**How do I calculate mine?** Battery kWh times your rate, divided by 0.9 for charging losses. Your pack size is on the spec sheet.

**Should I charge to 100?** For daily use, usually not; an 80 to 90 percent cap is gentler on the battery. Save full charges for trips.

**Why does it cost more than capacity times rate?** Charging loses about 10 percent as heat, so you pay for a bit more than lands in the battery.

**Does a bigger battery cost more per mile?** No. A bigger battery costs more per full charge but delivers more miles, so cost per mile depends on efficiency, not pack size.

**How many miles does a full charge give?** Roughly your battery kWh times your efficiency, so a 60 kWh pack at 3.5 mi/kWh is about 210 miles.

## The bottom line

A full 0-to-100 home charge costs about 8 to 20 dollars for most EVs at average rates, roughly half that overnight on an off-peak plan, and it is simply your battery capacity times your rate plus charging losses. But it overstates real life, since most owners add only a day's driving each night and cap daily charging for battery health.
