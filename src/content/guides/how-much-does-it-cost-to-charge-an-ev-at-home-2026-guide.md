The honest answer to what it costs to charge an EV at home is that most drivers spend somewhere around 35 to 60 dollars a month, but the number that matters is your own, and it comes from a formula simple enough to do on a napkin. Home charging cost is just the electricity you add multiplied by your rate, nudged up about ten percent for charging losses. Everything else, the model comparisons, the monthly estimates, the savings-versus-gas headlines, is that one equation dressed up. This guide gives you the formula, works it at the mid-2026 national-average electricity rate, and then shows exactly which levers move your cost up or down, so you leave able to estimate your own bill rather than trusting a stranger on the internet.

## The formula, once

Two numbers drive everything: your electricity rate and your car efficiency. Your rate is printed on your utility bill as the price per kWh. Your efficiency, in miles per kWh, comes off your dashboard or the EPA sticker, and for most EVs it lands between 3 and 4 miles per kWh, with efficient sedans nearer 4 and trucks nearer 2. Cost per mile is simply your rate divided by your efficiency. That single ratio is the cleanest way to compare any EV to any gas car, and it is what the [Sim-Lab charging calculator](/en/sim-lab/charging-cost/) computes for your exact inputs.

## What the national average actually is

As of mid-2026, the US average residential electricity rate is about 18.8 cents per kWh according to federal energy data, and it has been climbing, up roughly 7 percent over the prior year. But an average almost nobody pays: rates run near 12 cents in the cheapest states like North Dakota and past 40 cents in Hawaii, with California around 35 cents. So the same EV can cost three or four times as much to charge depending only on where you plug in. This is why we distrust any single headline figure and teach the formula instead, because your state rate changes the answer more than your choice of car does.

## A worked monthly estimate

Put real numbers to it. The average American drives roughly 1,000 to 1,200 miles a month. Take 1,100 miles in a 3.5 mile-per-kWh EV: that is about 314 kWh into the battery, or roughly 350 kWh from the wall after losses. At the national-average 18.8 cents, that is about 66 dollars a month. Drop to an off-peak rate near 9 cents and the same driving falls to roughly 32 dollars. Push to a 35-cent high-rate state and it climbs past 120 dollars. The 35-to-60-dollar figure you see quoted assumes average rates and average miles; your mileage and your rate slide it in either direction, which our [per-month guide](/how-much-it-costs-to-charge-an-ev-per-month-real-bills-explained/) breaks down against real bills.

The single biggest lever you control is when you charge. Many utilities offer time-of-use plans where overnight power costs far less than daytime, often 40 to 60 percent less, and since most home charging happens while you sleep, shifting to off-peak is close to free money, a topic our [time-of-use guide](/smart-charging-time-of-use-tou-2026-home-charging-optimization-guide/) covers in full. The other big one is avoiding public DC fast charging for daily needs, since at 30 to 50 cents per kWh it costs two to three times what home charging does. Vehicle efficiency and winter cold move the number too, just less dramatically.

## Home versus public, briefly

Because roughly 80 percent of EV charging happens at home for most owners, your real-world cost tracks the home rate closely even if you occasionally fast-charge on a road trip. Public Level 2 and DC fast stations carry overhead that home charging does not, so they cost more per kWh, sometimes enough that a heavy fast-charging habit erases much of the EV savings. The practical takeaway is to charge at home overnight for daily driving and treat fast charging as a road-trip tool, which our [home-versus-public comparison](/home-charging-vs-public-charging-cost-comparison-2026-guide/) quantifies.

## How it compares to gas

The comparison that motivates most buyers is stark at average prices. With gas near 3.80 dollars a gallon in a 30-mpg car, gasoline runs about 13 cents a mile, while home EV charging at the national-average rate runs about 5 cents a mile, roughly a third to half the cost. Over 12,000 miles a year that difference is commonly 700 to 1,200 dollars saved on fuel, more in cheap-electricity states and less in the priciest ones. Hawaii is the rare place the math tightens, though its gas is also expensive. Everywhere else on the mainland, home charging wins clearly.

## A note on rising rates

One reason to compute your own number rather than trust an old estimate is that electricity prices have been climbing steadily. National-average residential rates rose roughly 7 percent over the past year and about 25 percent over four years, driven by grid modernization, natural gas prices, and surging demand from data centers and electrification. That trend means a charging cost you calculated two years ago understates today's, and it will likely keep drifting upward. The practical response is not alarm but habit: charging off-peak insulates you from much of the increase, since off-peak rates tend to stay low even as flat rates rise, and it locks in the cheapest window your utility offers. Recompute your cost yearly with your current rate, and lean on off-peak charging to keep the number down as the broader price of electricity continues to rise.

## Quick answers

**What is the one-line answer?** Around 35 to 60 dollars a month at average rates and average miles, but compute your own: kWh added times your rate.

**What rate should I use?** The price per kWh on your own bill. The national average near 18.8 cents is only a placeholder until you find yours.

**How do I cut it most?** Shift charging to off-peak hours on a time-of-use plan; that is the biggest lever most homeowners control.

**Does fast charging count?** Only for the sessions you use it. At home you pay your residential rate; public DC fast charging costs two to three times more.

## The bottom line

Home EV charging costs whatever your electricity rate times your energy use says it does, and for average rates and average driving that lands near 35 to 60 dollars a month, a third to half the cost of gasoline. Your state rate is the biggest variable, and off-peak charging is the biggest lever you control.
