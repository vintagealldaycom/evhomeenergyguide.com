Installing a smart panel is not a weekend project, and understanding that clearly is the best thing you can do before one lands in your home. A smart panel replaces or supplements the main load center that feeds every circuit in the house, which means the work happens on live service conductors, requires a licensed electrician and a permit almost everywhere, and includes a power-off window while the swap happens. None of that should scare you off; it just sets expectations. This guide walks the whole arc, from the first site visit to the moment the app lights up, so you know what a competent installation looks like and can tell a smooth job from a rushed one.

## Stage one: the site visit that sets the price

A good installation starts with a real inspection, not an online estimate. The electrician should open your existing panel, confirm your service amperage, look at the panel location and Wi-Fi reach, and check for anything an inspector will flag. This visit is where the true scope emerges, and it is where you find out whether your project is a clean swap or whether it triggers a service upgrade, which our [costs guide](/smart-panel-costs-2026-guide-what-homeowners-really-pay-for-the-upgrade/) covers in depth. Insist on an itemized quote that separates the smart panel from any service or code work, because that is the only way to compare bids honestly.

## Stage two: permits are not optional

In nearly every jurisdiction, replacing a load center requires a permit and a final inspection. This protects you: it means a qualified inspector confirms the work is safe before it is signed off, and it keeps your home insurance and future sale clean. An electrician who suggests skipping the permit is a red flag, not a bargain. Depending on your utility, the power-off window may also require the utility to pull the meter, which the electrician coordinates. Permit and inspection fees are usually modest relative to the job, but they are a real line in the budget.

## Stage three: the power-off swap

The swap itself is the visible part. For a full replacement, the electrician kills power at the service, removes the old panel, and moves each branch circuit into the new smart panel, labeling as they go. For an add-on smart subpanel, the job is smaller because only the circuits you want to control get moved. Careful, accurate circuit labeling is the unglamorous step that determines whether your app is useful later, so it is worth confirming the electrician labels every circuit clearly rather than leaving it for you to reverse-engineer.

## Stage four and five: inspection, then commissioning

After the physical work, an inspector reviews it. Once it passes, the panel is final and you move to commissioning: connecting the panel to your home Wi-Fi and setting up the app. This is where circuit labels become live data, and it is the moment to verify each labeled circuit actually reads the load you expect by turning things on and watching the app respond. A common real-world snag here is weak Wi-Fi at the panel, which often lives in a garage or basement corner, so a mesh node or a wired connection near the panel can save you support headaches. If control and scheduling are why you bought the panel, our [EV charging guide](/smart-panels-for-ev-charging/) shows how to set those rules up.

## Choosing the right electrician

The installer matters as much as the hardware. Smart panels are a newer category, and not every excellent electrician has wired one, so it is fair to ask directly whether they have installed the specific panel you are considering and how many. A good sign is an electrician who talks fluently about commissioning the app and labeling circuits, not just the physical swap, because the software side is where a poorly executed job shows up months later. Ask for references on similar installs, confirm they will pull the permit, and make sure the quote itemizes the panel, labor, permit, and any service or code work separately so you can compare bids on equal terms.

## Living with the panel after install

The installation is not really finished when the inspector leaves; it is finished when the app reflects your home accurately and you know how to use it. Spend the first week verifying that each labeled circuit reads the load you expect, setting any priority rules for load management, and configuring schedules if off-peak charging is a goal. This is also when small issues surface, a mislabeled circuit, a flaky Wi-Fi connection at the panel, a schedule that did not save, and it is far easier to have the electrician address them while the job is fresh than months later. Treat the week after commissioning as part of the project, not an afterthought, and the panel will earn its cost through the visibility and control you actually configured rather than the features you never set up.

## Quick answers

**How long does it take?** A clean full-panel swap is a half-day to full-day job; add-on subpanels are shorter. A triggered service upgrade adds time.

**Will my power be off?** Yes, for a full replacement, typically several hours. Plan around refrigeration and any medical equipment.

**Can I install it myself?** No. This is live service-panel work that requires a licensed electrician and, in nearly all areas, a permit and inspection.

**What is the most overlooked step?** Circuit labeling and Wi-Fi reach at the panel. Both are cheap to get right and expensive to fix later.

## The bottom line

A smart panel installation is a permitted, inspected, electrician-led job with a real power-off window, not a DIY afternoon. The difference between a smooth project and a frustrating one comes down to three things: an itemized quote after a genuine inspection, careful circuit labeling, and solid Wi-Fi at the panel. Get those right and the app you use every day afterward will actually reflect your home.
