Let's set the honest baseline first: the lithium-ion packs powering your drill and mower are engineered products with protection circuits, tested to safety standards, and the overwhelming majority live their whole lives without a single dramatic moment. You don't need to fear them. What the fire statistics actually show — and residential lithium battery fires have risen sharply in recent years — is that failures cluster around a short list of specific behaviors: damaged packs kept in service, knockoff batteries and chargers, charging on soft or flammable surfaces, and charging unattended. Which is good news, because a short list of behaviors is a fixable thing. This guide covers what can go wrong, the handful of rules that prevent nearly all of it, how to recognize a failing pack early, and what to do — and never do — if one lets go.

## What actually goes wrong: thermal runaway in plain language

A lithium-ion cell stores a lot of energy in a small package with a flammable electrolyte inside. If a cell is damaged, internally shorted, overcharged past its limits, or overheated, it can enter thermal runaway: the cell heats itself, the heat accelerates the chemical reaction, pressure builds, and the cell vents hot flammable gas that usually ignites. In a multi-cell pack, one runaway cell cooks its neighbors, which is why these fires escalate fast, burn aggressively, and can reignite after they seem out.

Three things make tool packs specifically manageable: the cells are cylindrical (the most rugged format), the pack has a battery management system supervising every charge, and reputable chargers negotiate with that BMS. Every rule below exists to keep that protection chain intact — and every risky behavior is some way of breaking it.

## The prevention rules, ranked by how much they matter

**Buy real, buy listed.** Use the manufacturer's packs and chargers, or at minimum products carrying a genuine UL or ETL listing. The bargain third-party pack is where the fire statistics live: cheap cells, missing protection circuitry, chargers that don't stop. Fire-safety agencies from the CPSC to the USFA hammer this point above all others, and it's also the recall angle — check your packs against the CPSC recall database once in a while; tool and battery recalls happen more often than people realize.

**Charge attended, on a hard surface, at room temperature.** The national fire guidance is consistent: charge on a hard non-combustible surface (not the couch, not a cardboard box, not a pile of shop rags), don't charge while you sleep or while nobody's home, and unplug or remove packs when charging finishes rather than letting them live on the charger. Temperature bounds matter too — roughly 40–104°F for charging — and the full room-by-room detail is in the [safe charging guide](/how-to-charge-cordless-tool-batteries-safely/).

**Store cool, dry, and partial.** Heat ages cells and is the environmental risk factor; a pack on a sun-baked windowsill or a 120°F attic shelf is being pushed toward the exact condition you're trying to avoid. Storage wants somewhere temperate, away from flammables, packs at 40–50% charge — the whole approach is the [battery-first storage guide](/how-to-store-cordless-power-tools-safely-battery-first-approach/), and households with many packs should read the [fire-resistant battery cabinet guide](/how-to-build-a-fire-resistant-battery-storage-cabinet/) for the containment layer.

**Protect the terminals.** A loose pack in a toolbox with wrenches, screws or keys can short across its terminals — one of the oldest and most preventable failure paths. Packs travel and store with terminals covered or nothing metal nearby.

**Don't abuse, don't ignore abuse.** Drops onto concrete, crushing, punctures, and soaking are all internal-damage events even when the case looks fine. A pack that took a hard hit earns a probation period: watched during its next charges, retired at the first odd sign.

The middle rows are where homeowners save themselves. A swollen pack that gets retired is a non-event; a swollen pack that goes back on the charger "one more time" is how the fire reports start. If you catch a pack at the swelling-or-odor stage and it's cool and stable, move it outdoors or to a non-combustible spot (concrete floor, metal container with sand) away from the house while you arrange disposal — and don't tape over vents, don't puncture, don't submerge it.

## If a battery fire happens

<div class="callout warn"><span class="co-icon">!</span><div><strong>People first, always.</strong> Lithium battery fires escalate fast, produce toxic smoke, can be explosive, and reignite after appearing out. National fire guidance is blunt: leave the area and call 911. If - and only if - the fire is tiny, caught instantly, and you can act without risk, water is actually a reasonable tool on a small lithium-ion fire because cooling is what stops cell-to-cell spread, and an ABC extinguisher can knock down surrounding flames. But no object in your garage is worth the attempt. Evacuate, call, and warn firefighters it's a lithium battery - and expect the pack to be treated as reignition-capable for hours afterward.</div></div>

Two placement habits shrink the stakes before anything ever happens: a working smoke alarm covering the garage or wherever charging lives, and a charging spot that doesn't block your exit path. Both cost almost nothing.

## The end of life: recycling done right

Every tool battery ends somewhere, and the trash is the one place it must never be — packs crushed in garbage trucks and transfer stations are a leading ignition source in the waste industry, and most regions ban it outright.

Healthy, intact packs are easy: major hardware retailers host free drop-off bins run by the national battery-recycling network (the program long known as Call2Recycle). Tape the terminals or bag each pack, carry them upright, done — the materials get recovered and the pack stays out of the waste stream. Dead-but-intact packs from any brand are accepted.

Damaged, swollen or recalled packs are a different channel, and this is the distinction people miss: the retail bins don't take them, because damaged lithium batteries require special handling under transport regulations. Those go to your municipal household hazardous waste facility — call ahead, tell them what you're bringing, and transport the pack secured, cool, and cushioned in something non-flammable like sand or kitty litter. If the whole tool is being retired, packs come out first; the tool body is ordinary metal/e-waste recycling once the battery is separated.

## Quick answers

**Are cordless tool batteries actually dangerous?** Intact, genuine packs used with their own chargers are very safe. The risk concentrates in damaged packs kept in service, counterfeit batteries and chargers, and unattended charging on flammable surfaces — all avoidable.

**Is it safe to leave batteries on the charger overnight?** No — fire-safety agencies specifically advise against charging while sleeping or away, and staying on the charger at 100% ages the pack anyway. Charge while you're around; remove when done.

**What do I do with a swollen battery?** Stop using and charging it, move it somewhere non-combustible away from the house, and take it to household hazardous waste — not the trash, not the retail recycling bin. Don't puncture it or tape the vents.

**Can I put out a battery fire with water?** Small and caught instantly, water cools cells and can help — but the official guidance is to evacuate and call 911, because these fires escalate and reignite. Never treat a growing battery fire as a DIY project.

**Where do I recycle old tool batteries?** Free drop-off bins at major hardware retailers for intact packs (terminals taped). Damaged or swollen packs go through your household hazardous waste program instead.

## The bottom line

Battery safety is a short contract: genuine listed equipment, attended charging on hard surfaces at room temperature, cool half-charged storage away from flammables, terminals protected, and immediate retirement of any pack that swells, stinks or runs hot. Hold those lines and the technology holds its end. And when a pack's time comes, the taped-terminal trip to the drop-off bin — or the hazardous-waste run for a damaged one — is the last safety habit that makes all the others complete.
