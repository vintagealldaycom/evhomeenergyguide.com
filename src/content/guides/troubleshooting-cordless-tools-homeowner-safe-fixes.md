A cordless tool that suddenly won't run feels dead in a way corded tools never did — no motor hum, no half-hearted spin, just nothing. But here's what a decade of repair-counter statistics quietly shows: most "dead" cordless tools aren't broken. They're locked out by their own protection electronics, wearing dirty contacts, or holding a battery that's the real patient. Before you buy anything or open anything, run this sequence. It's ordered from most likely to least likely, and it stops cold at the line where homeowner repair ends and hazard begins.

## First, prove which half is sick

A cordless tool is two machines: the tool and the pack. Never troubleshoot both at once.

Swap in a second battery you know is charged. Tool runs? The problem was the battery — head to the [battery won't charge guide](/why-your-battery-wont-charge-troubleshooting-guide-for-lawn-tools/) and stop reading here. Tool still dead with a known-good pack? Now put the suspect battery in a different tool — a work light is perfect because it draws almost nothing. Battery runs the light? Battery's fine, the tool is your patient. This two-swap test takes ninety seconds and it's the single most valuable habit in cordless diagnostics, because people routinely replace tools with bad batteries and batteries with bad tools.

Only one battery in the house? That's your cue that you're under-equipped for diagnosis, not that the tool is dead — borrow a pack from a neighbor on the same platform before spending money.

## The five-minute physical checks

**Seat the battery like you mean it.** Packs must click home fully; a pack sitting one millimeter proud makes intermittent contact that shows up as a tool that stutters or dies when you tilt it. Pull it off, look at the rails for debris, and slide it on until it locks.

**Clean the contacts.** The battery terminals and the tool's mating contacts collect sawdust, pocket lint and — in damp garages — a dull oxide film that adds resistance. Wipe both sets with a dry cloth; for visible grime or corrosion, a cotton swab barely dampened with isopropyl alcohol, then dry. Don't file or sand contacts — you'll strip plating and make corrosion permanent.

**Check the trigger and switches.** Forward/reverse selectors have a center lock-off position, and a selector bumped to center is the most embarrassing "dead drill" cause there is. Work the trigger a dozen times; a scratchy, intermittent response points to dust in the switch, which sometimes clears with a few sharp trigger cycles and a blast of dry air into the handle vents — from the outside, tool unpowered.

**Look for the blockage.** Saws with jammed guards, string trimmers wrapped in fishing-line-grade tangles, mowers with packed decks — plenty of tools have safety interlocks that refuse to start when the business end can't move. Battery out first, always, then clear the mechanism.

The first row deserves its own paragraph, because it fools everyone. Modern cordless tools — and their batteries — carry protection electronics that shut things down before damage happens: too hot, too much current, voltage sagging too low. A tool that dies mid-cut and works again ten minutes later didn't glitch; it protected itself. The real question is why. Dull blade or bit forcing the motor to grind? Vents packed with dust so the motor can't shed heat? Battery too small for the tool — a compact 1.5Ah pack asked to run a circular saw will sag and trip constantly where a 5Ah pack cruises? Fix the cause and the "cutting out" problem evaporates. Vent cleaning alone — vacuum, never compressed air blasted inward — resolves a remarkable share of these cases, and it's a core habit from the [cordless tool maintenance routine](/cordless-tool-maintenance-for-homeowners/).

## The stuck chuck and other mechanical classics

A drill chuck that won't release its bit isn't a motor problem, and it almost never needs a repair shop. Set the clutch to drill mode, grip the chuck collar with one hand (or a strap wrench for the truly stuck), and run the drill briefly in reverse at low speed. For a keyless chuck seized by grit, a drop of light oil at the jaw opening, a few open-close cycles, then wipe everything — a gummy chuck is usually a dirty chuck. Impact drivers with bits stuck in the collet respond to pulling the collar forward firmly while wiggling the bit; corrosion in the hex socket is the usual villain, and the fix is cleaning, not force.

Brushed motors — common on older and budget tools — have one genuinely serviceable part: the carbon brushes. If an older tool sparks heavily in the vents, smells faintly electrical, and runs weaker and weaker, brushes worn to stubs are the classic cause. Many tools have external brush caps you can unscrew; replacement brushes cost little and the swap is a legitimate homeowner job with the battery removed. Brushless tools (most tools sold in the last several years) have no brushes — their equivalent failures are electronic and are not user-serviceable.

## Where homeowner repair ends

<div class="callout warn"><span class="co-icon">!</span><div><strong>Three hard stops.</strong> Never open a battery pack - the cells inside can short, vent or ignite, and a pack is not repairable at home under any circumstances. Never bypass a safety interlock, guard switch or trigger lock to "test" a tool. And never keep running a tool that grinds, smokes or smells burnt - a failing gearbox or motor gets more dangerous, not better.</div></div>

There's also a money line worth naming. Trigger switches, chucks and brushes are cheap parts. But if a brushless tool's control board or motor dies out of warranty, the repair quote routinely lands near the price of a new bare tool — and "bare tool" is the key phrase, because your batteries and charger carry over within your platform. Check the warranty first, though: several major brands cover tools for three to five years, and a surprising number of "dead tool" stories end with a free warranty replacement that the owner almost didn't bother claiming.

## Quick answers

**Why does my cordless drill stop and start?** Usually a battery not fully clicked in, dirty contacts, or a worn trigger switch — in that order. The tilt test tells you a lot: if it dies when the tool changes angle, it's a connection, not a motor.

**Why does my tool die under load but the battery shows charged?** Protection circuits are tripping — from heat, a dull blade, blocked vents, or a battery too small for the job. An aging pack can also show "full" at rest but collapse under load; test with a bigger, newer pack.

**Can I fix a cordless tool switch myself?** Replacement switches exist for many models and the job is doable for someone comfortable with small electronics — battery out, photos as you go. If soldering isn't your thing, weigh the repair against a bare-tool replacement.

**Is a tool that smells burnt ever okay?** No. A faint warm-electronics smell after hard use can be normal; an acrid burnt smell, smoke, or heavy visible sparking means stop. Motors and boards fail unsafe, not graceful.

**My brand-new tool doesn't work — now what?** Don't troubleshoot it beyond the swap test. New-tool failures are exactly what retailer returns and warranties exist for, and opening anything can void both.

## The bottom line

Split the diagnosis (tool or battery), do the ninety-second physical checks, and respect what protection electronics are telling you before assuming failure. The genuinely fixable stuff — contacts, seating, vents, chucks, brushes on older tools — costs nothing or nearly nothing. The genuinely broken stuff announces itself with grinding, smoke and fault codes, and the answer there is warranty first, bare tool second, and never, ever, opening a battery.
