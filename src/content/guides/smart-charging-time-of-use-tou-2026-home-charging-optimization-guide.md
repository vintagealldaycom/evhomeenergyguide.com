The single biggest lever most EV owners have over their charging cost is not the car or the charger but the clock. Time-of-use electricity rates charge different prices at different hours, and because nearly all home charging happens overnight while you sleep, shifting it into the cheap off-peak window can cut the cost of every mile by 40 to 60 percent for almost no effort. This guide explains what time-of-use rates are in plain terms, why EV owners benefit from them more than almost anyone, and how to shift your charging to off-peak automatically so the savings happen without you thinking about it.

A time-of-use plan simply prices electricity by how busy the grid is. During peak hours, when everyone is home running appliances, power is expensive; overnight, when demand collapses, it is cheap. The gap is large: peak rates can be two to three times off-peak rates on the same plan. A flat-rate plan, by contrast, charges one price all day. The whole strategy for EV owners is to move a big flexible load, charging, out of the expensive window and into the cheap one, which our [cost-to-charge guide](/how-much-does-it-cost-to-charge-an-ev-at-home-2026-guide/) identifies as the top cost lever.

## Why EV owners save the most

<div class="art-viz">
  <div class="callout ok"><span class="co-icon"></span><div><strong>EV charging is the perfect off-peak load.</strong> It is large, flexible, and naturally happens overnight. No other household load lines up so well with cheap off-peak hours, which is why EV owners benefit from time-of-use plans more than almost anyone.</div></div>
</div>

Most households cannot shift much of their usage; you cook dinner at dinnertime and run the AC when it is hot. EV charging is different: it is a large load that does not care when it happens, as long as the car is full by morning. That makes it the ideal candidate for off-peak pricing. A driver who moves charging from a 20-cent peak to a 9-cent off-peak rate cuts charging cost by more than half, turning a 60-dollar month into under 30 dollars. Because the load is both big and perfectly flexible, EV owners capture more time-of-use savings than a typical household ever could.

## Shifting to off-peak automatically

The beauty of off-peak charging is that you set it once and forget it. Nearly every EV and most smart chargers let you schedule charging to begin at your off-peak start time, so you plug in whenever you get home and the car waits for cheap power on its own. Some utilities offer dedicated EV rate plans with especially low overnight rates, sometimes with a separate meter. A [smart panel](/smart-panels-for-ev-charging/) can enforce this at the circuit level and combine it with load management. The key is automation: once the schedule is set, you capture the savings every night without a single decision.

Not every home benefits equally, so it is worth a quick check. If you can shift your charging and much of your other flexible usage off-peak, a time-of-use plan almost always saves money for an EV owner. If your household runs heavy loads during peak hours and cannot move them, the higher peak rate might offset the savings, so compare before switching. For most EV owners, though, the overnight charging alone tips the balance decisively toward time-of-use, which is why utilities increasingly design plans around it.

## A word on grid benefits

Beyond your own savings, there is a reason utilities actively encourage off-peak charging, and understanding it reassures you the discount is durable rather than a temporary promotion. The grid must be built to handle its busiest moment, the peak, and every load added during peak hours pushes toward expensive new capacity. Overnight, that same infrastructure sits underused. When EV owners charge off-peak, they use existing capacity that would otherwise be idle, which is cheaper for the utility to serve, so the low off-peak rate reflects a genuine cost difference, not a gimmick. This alignment of interests, your lower bill and the grid's smoother demand, is why time-of-use and dedicated EV rate plans keep expanding rather than disappearing. It also means charging off-peak is a small civic good on top of a personal saving: you are helping flatten demand and defer costly grid expansion. For the homeowner, the takeaway is simply that the off-peak discount rests on real economics and is worth building a permanent habit around.

## Quick answers

**How much does off-peak charging save?** Commonly 40 to 60 percent on charging cost, since off-peak rates often run less than half of peak rates.

**Do I have to charge manually at night?** No. Set a schedule in your car or charger once, and it charges in the off-peak window automatically.

**Is a time-of-use plan always better?** For most EV owners, yes, because charging shifts easily. Compare if your household runs heavy peak-hour loads you cannot move.

**What is a dedicated EV rate?** A special utility plan with very low overnight rates for EV charging, sometimes on a separate meter. Ask your utility.

**Will off-peak charging finish my car by morning?** Almost always. The overnight window is long enough to replace a day of driving several times over at Level 2 speeds.

**Does off-peak charging hurt the battery?** No. Charging overnight at moderate Level 2 power is gentle; it is repeated fast charging, not off-peak home charging, that stresses a pack.

## The bottom line

Time-of-use charging is the closest thing to free money in EV ownership: overnight power often costs less than half of peak power, and since charging is a big flexible load that naturally happens at night, shifting it off-peak cuts your cost per mile by 40 to 60 percent. Set your car or charger to start in the off-peak window once, confirm it on your bill, and the savings arrive every night automatically. For most owners, getting on the right plan is the highest-value move available.
