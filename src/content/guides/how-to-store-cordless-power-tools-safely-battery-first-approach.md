Here's a small reframe that will save you hundreds of dollars over a decade of tool ownership: you don't store cordless tools. You store batteries that happen to have tools attached. The drill body is a rugged lump of motor and plastic that would survive twenty years in a damp shed; the pack clipped to it is a perishable chemical device that's aging every single day, at a rate set almost entirely by two numbers — its temperature and its state of charge. Get those two numbers right during storage and your packs last years longer. Get them wrong and you'll replace batteries that never did a hard day's work. That's the battery-first approach: sort every storage decision by what the pack needs, and let the tool ride along.

## The two numbers that decide everything

**Temperature.** Lithium-ion chemistry ages fastest at temperature extremes. Heat is the silent one — cell degradation reactions accelerate with temperature, so a pack that summers on a 110°F garage shelf or in a sun-baked truck box is being slow-cooked even while "resting." Cold is the sharp one — packs stored through hard-freezing winters lose real capacity (repair-bench reports describe packs losing a noticeable slice of capacity after a single deep-frozen winter), casings and seals get brittle, and the freeze-thaw cycle invites condensation onto terminals and boards. The comfortable band for storage is roughly 40–70°F, and the practical translation is one sentence: batteries store where you'd be comfortable, which usually means indoors — a basement shelf, a utility-room cabinet, a closet — not the unconditioned garage in January or the shed in July.

**State of charge.** Packs age fastest sitting at the extremes: parked at 100% (which is what living on a charger means) stresses cells continuously, while sitting near 0% risks the slide into deep-discharge lockout — the "charger says defective" spring ritual covered in the [won't-charge guide](/why-your-battery-wont-charge-troubleshooting-guide-for-lawn-tools/). The storage sweet spot is 40–50% — on most packs' fuel gauges, two bars out of four. It's not a coincidence that shipping regulations require lithium batteries transported by air to sit near 30% charge: partial charge is both the longevity setting and the lower-energy, safer setting.

## Packs come out of tools

The third pillar of battery-first storage: for anything beyond day-to-day rotation, the battery comes off the tool. Four separate reasons stack up. Tools have tiny phantom draws (LED circuits, electronics) that slowly drain an attached pack toward the deep-discharge cliff. A tool with a pack attached is a tool that can start — in a moving box, in a kid's hands, under a pile in a bag. Detached packs can be stored in the good indoor spot while bulky tools stay on the garage wall, which is the whole geographic trick of this approach. And detached terminals can be protected: stored with contacts facing up or covered, never loose in a drawer of screwdrivers and loose screws where something metal can bridge them — the shorted-in-the-toolbox failure is ancient, and it's still happening.

The daily-driver exception is fine: the drill you use every week keeps its pack. Battery-first is a storage rule, not a usage tax. The dividing line worth drawing in your head is about a month — gear you'll touch within a month can stay assembled and ready; anything sleeping longer than that gets the full treatment, because a month is roughly where phantom drain and self-discharge start doing arithmetic against you.

## Where the fleet lives

The ideal arrangement splits the gear by what each piece actually needs. Tools — the bodies — are indifferent: garage wall hooks, shelves, cases, anywhere dry, following ordinary tool-care sense (a wipe-down before they go up, per the [maintenance routine](/cordless-tool-maintenance-for-homeowners/)). Batteries — the perishables — live indoors on a dedicated shelf or, for bigger fleets, in a [ventilated fire-resistant cabinet](/how-to-build-a-fire-resistant-battery-storage-cabinet/): spaced so packs don't touch, off metal shelving or on a plastic/wood liner, away from sunlight, flammables and anything that drips. Chargers live at the [charging station](/how-to-set-up-a-charging-station-for-cordless-tools/) — and the packs don't live with them. The station is a kitchen, not a bedroom.

## The seasonal transitions

**Going into winter:** every pack that won't be used gets charged or drained to ~50%, pulled off its tool, and moved indoors. The tools winter in the garage happily. Anything that must stay in a cold garage (say, at a seasonal property) goes into an insulated container to buffer the swings — while understanding that's harm reduction, not a solution, and hard-freeze storage still costs capacity.

**Coming out of winter:** cold packs warm indoors for a couple of hours before charging — charging below about 40°F causes permanent internal damage — then charge fully and go to work. A pack that draws a fault light after proper warming gets the [revival ladder](/how-to-revive-a-dead-lithium-battery-safe-methods-only/), gently.

**Through summer:** the enemy flips to heat. Packs don't ride in car trunks between jobs, don't sun on windowsills, and if the garage runs brutally hot, the indoor battery shelf earns its keep twice a year instead of once.

## Quick answers

**What charge level should tool batteries be stored at?** 40–50% — about two of four bars on the gauge. Not full, not empty. Top back to half during long storage if the gauge drops low.

**Is it bad to store batteries in the garage?** In mild climates, a garage that stays roughly 40–70°F is fine. Where garages freeze or bake, batteries store indoors — the tools can stay, the packs move.

**Should I take batteries off tools for storage?** Yes, for anything stored more than a few weeks: it stops phantom drain, prevents accidental starts, and lets packs live in the good climate while tools stay on the wall.

**Can I store batteries on the charger so they're always ready?** No — that parks them at 100%, the aging-fastest state, and adds pointless always-plugged-in exposure. Store at half-charge; charge before use.

**How long can a battery sit unused?** Stored at half-charge and room temperature, many months to a year with just a seasonal top-up. Stored empty or frozen, one winter can be enough to kill it.

## The bottom line

Battery-first storage is three rules and a calendar reminder: packs at 40–50%, packs at people-temperature, packs off the tools with terminals protected — and a seasonal check to top the sleepers back to half. The tools will forgive any storage sins you commit against them; the batteries forgive none. Store for the pack, and the most expensive consumable in your workshop quietly stops being consumed.
