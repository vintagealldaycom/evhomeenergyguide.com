People compare smart panels and subpanels because both get pitched as the answer to "I'm out of room in my panel" — but they solve genuinely different problems, and confusing them wastes money. A subpanel is a satellite breaker box fed from your main panel; it adds physical circuit slots, usually in a location far from the main. A smart panel is a main-panel replacement that adds metering and control. One buys you *space and reach*; the other buys you *visibility and management*. Figuring out which you actually need starts with naming the real constraint.

Note the subtlety: a "smart load center" is just a smart panel by another name — vendors use "load center" and "panel" interchangeably. So "smart panel vs smart load center" isn't a real choice; it's the same product. The real fork is **dumb subpanel vs smart main panel**, and that fork is about *why* you feel out of room.

## When the subpanel is the right call

If your constraint is slots or distance — you've filled the main panel with circuits, or you're wiring a detached garage or a home addition — a subpanel is the clean, inexpensive, universally-understood solution. Any electrician can install one, there's nothing to fail electronically, and it does exactly what it promises. Layering a smart panel onto a pure space-or-reach problem is overkill.

## When the smart panel is the right call

If your constraint is *capacity* — you want to add an [EV charger](/smart-panel-vs-traditional-panel/), a heat pump, or a second big load and your service can't obviously handle it — the smart panel's automated [load management](/smart-panel-vs-load-management/) can let those loads coexist by never running full-tilt at the same instant. It squeezes more useful work out of the amperage you already have, which a subpanel physically cannot do. You'd also choose a smart panel if visibility and app control matter to you on their own.

## You can also have both

These aren't mutually exclusive. A common layout is a smart main panel handling metering, control, and load management for the house, with a plain subpanel downstream feeding a distant workshop. The smart panel manages the big picture; the subpanel just extends reach. If you're building out a garage *and* adding an EV charger on a tight service, that combination is often exactly right.

## A worked example: the garage EV charger

Picture the most common version of this decision. You have a 100-amp service, a nearly full panel, and you want to add a 48-amp Level 2 charger in the garage. Your first instinct — and often your electrician's first quote — is a subpanel in the garage to host the charger circuit. It sounds right: more slots, power out where you need it.

But run the numbers. A subpanel doesn't create amperage; it hands your existing service down to a new box. Add a continuous 48-amp charger draw on top of a house that already runs a heat pump, an electric range, and a dryer, and your peak demand can blow past what 100-amp service is rated to carry. The subpanel gave you a place to land the circuit, not the capacity to run it. At that point the electrician's next quote is the expensive one: a full service upgrade to 200 amps, trenching and utility coordination included.

Now the smart-panel path. A [smart panel's load management](/smart-panel-vs-load-management/) lets that same 48-amp charger draw full power when the house is quiet — overnight, when you actually charge — and automatically throttle back the instant the range and dryer fire up together. Peak demand never exceeds what your 100-amp service can carry, because the panel guarantees it. You fit the charger without the service upgrade. The smart panel costs more than a subpanel, but if it replaces a service upgrade you were otherwise going to buy, it can come out even or ahead — and you get whole-home visibility as a bonus.

The lesson generalizes: reach for a subpanel when you're out of *space*, and reach for a smart panel when you're out of *capacity*. The garage-charger case fools people because it looks like a space problem and is actually a capacity problem in disguise.

## Quick answers

**Is a smart load center different from a smart panel?** No — "load center" and "panel" are two names for the same box. A smart load center is a smart panel.

**Will a subpanel let me add an EV charger?** Only if your service has spare amperage. If it doesn't, a subpanel won't help; a [smart panel's load management](/smart-panel-vs-load-management/) might.

**Can I put a subpanel downstream of a smart panel?** Yes, that's a normal and useful setup — smart brains at the main, extra reach at the subpanel.

**Which is cheaper?** A subpanel, by a wide margin. It's the right choice whenever your problem is genuinely just space or distance.

**Does adding a subpanel require a permit?** Almost always yes, and an inspection — it's branch-circuit work on your service. Treat it as a licensed-electrician job, not a weekend project.

## The bottom line

Name the constraint before you buy. Out of *slots* or need to reach a distant building? A subpanel, cheap and simple. Out of *capacity*, or you want visibility and control? A smart panel, which can fit more onto the same service and show you where every watt goes. "Smart load center" is marketing for the same smart panel — don't let the vocabulary talk you into paying twice for one idea.
