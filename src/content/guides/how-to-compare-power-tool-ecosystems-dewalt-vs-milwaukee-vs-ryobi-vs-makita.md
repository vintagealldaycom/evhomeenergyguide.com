The DeWalt-versus-Milwaukee-versus-Ryobi-versus-Makita argument has been running in workshops and forums for a solid decade, and it stays alive because everyone frames it as a contest with a winner. It isn't one. These are four ecosystems — battery systems with tool libraries attached — that made different bets about who they serve, and each bet paid off for its intended person. The useful comparison isn't "which is best," it's "which one's bets match your life," because once you buy in, the batteries lock the door behind you. Here's the honest map: what each system is, where it genuinely leads, where its fans quietly compromise, and who walks away happy. (If you haven't yet settled the bigger question of how to think about platforms at all — budget math, voltage marketing, the two-platform yard question — start with the [platform decision guide](/how-to-choose-the-right-battery-platform-homeowner-guide/); this page is the head-to-head it points to.)

## The lay of the land

One myth to clear immediately, since it colors this whole matchup: Milwaukee and Ryobi share a parent company (TTI), and DeWalt shares one with Craftsman and Black+Decker (Stanley Black & Decker) — but shared ownership is not shared engineering. These are distinct product lines built for different markets at different price points, and "Milwaukee is just expensive Ryobi" is false in both directions. Also worth restating here: the 18V/20V-MAX label difference is marketing math on the same nominal voltage. All four core platforms are electrical peers; the differences live in cells, motors, electronics and ecosystem strategy.

## Ryobi ONE+: the value ecosystem

Ryobi's bet: own the homeowner completely, on price and sheer variety. The ONE+ 18V line is the largest battery ecosystem going — three-hundred-plus tools on one pack, from drills to glue guns to fans to misters — with backward compatibility stretching back decades, batteries at roughly half premium-brand prices, and Home Depot as its everywhere-retail home. The compromise its fans accept: build quality tuned for weekends, not jobsites. Ryobi tools live shorter, harder-used lives in professional hands — which is fine, because professional hands aren't the customer. For the occasional-use homeowner, the honest analysis keeps landing the same place: capability-per-dollar nobody else touches, and the giant library means the weird tool you want in 2031 probably exists. Ryobi also fields 40V yard gear as a separate system — note that ONE+ and 40V packs don't interchange, a recurring buyer surprise.

## DeWalt 20V MAX: the broad middle that's everywhere

DeWalt's bet: be the default. The 20V MAX platform pairs pro-grade quality with the widest retail availability in North America — every major chain, the deepest parts-and-service reach — at pricing below the premium tier. Its structural ace is FlexVolt: dual-voltage batteries that run both 20V tools and 60V heavy tools (big saws, serious yard gear), letting one ecosystem stretch from a compact drill to a cordless table saw. The compromises: a product line so broad it spans from budget-grade to excellent (its cheapest tier earns its reputation), and naming conventions that genuinely confuse buyers. Who walks away happy: the regular DIYer and light pro who wants quality-without-ceremony, buys wherever's convenient, and might want big-tool power someday without a second platform.

## Milwaukee M18: the premium pro system

Milwaukee's bet: win the trades outright and let the premium price filter the customers. M18 FUEL is what commercial jobsites overwhelmingly run — refined ergonomics, protective electronics across tools and packs, an industry-benchmark five-year tool warranty, and pack technology (the FORGE high-output line) at the front of the field, with an M12 compact system alongside that pros adore. The compromises: the price premium is real — comparable kits run meaningfully above DeWalt's — and first-party retail is effectively Home Depot. The honest homeowner question is whether that premium buys anything a homeowner uses; for tools-twice-a-month life, mostly it buys pride of ownership. For daily users, heavy renovators and buy-once temperaments, it buys exactly what it claims.

## Makita LXT/XGT: the craftsman's pick

Makita's bet: engineering refinement over market blanket. LXT 18V is a vast, mature system (with a clever twin-18V trick powering 36V tools from paired packs), famous for motor quality, light weight per unit of power, and a fit-and-finish that woodworkers and finish carpenters gravitate toward. XGT is its separate 40V-class heavy platform for corded-class power. The compromises: a two-platform structure that forces a real choice (LXT and XGT packs don't mix), thinner North American retail presence and slower service outside metro areas, and premium-tier pricing. Who walks away happy: the deliberate buyer — often precision-work-oriented — who researches, orders, and values refinement over the corner-store convenience the other three sell.

Two cross-cutting notes the table can't hold. First, the battery-technology gap has narrowed dramatically: all four now ship advanced high-output pack lines (tabless-cell designs and equivalents), so "brand X's batteries are years ahead" is largely yesterday's argument — cell tech diffused across the field. Second, whatever you choose, the batteries are the marriage: the packs are where your money accumulates, their prices differ between these systems far more than tool prices do, and how you treat them ([charging habits](/how-to-charge-cordless-tool-batteries-safely/), [half-charge storage](/how-to-store-cordless-power-tools-safely-battery-first-approach/)) moves your ten-year cost more than the brand logo does.

## Quick answers

**Is Milwaukee just a more expensive Ryobi since TTI owns both?** No — shared parent, separate engineering, different markets. Milwaukee is built and priced for daily professional use; Ryobi for homeowner value. Neither is the other in disguise.

**Which ecosystem has the most tools?** Ryobi ONE+ by raw count (300+). Milwaukee counters with the deepest professional and specialty lineup; DeWalt's FlexVolt uniquely stretches one ecosystem into heavy 60V territory.

**Is DeWalt or Milwaukee better?** Neither, categorically — Milwaukee edges build refinement and warranty, DeWalt edges availability, price and dual-voltage reach. Daily users lean Milwaukee; most homeowners get everything they need from DeWalt for less.

**Why do people love Makita if it's harder to buy?** Motor and build refinement, especially per pound — it's the finish carpenter's repeated pick. Its buyers order deliberately and don't miss the big-box convenience.

**Do premium batteries from these brands last longer?** Premium packs deliver more power and shrug off heavy loads better, but longevity is mostly habits: any brand's pack lives long stored at half charge and charged at room temperature — and dies young on a hot charger.

## The bottom line

Four ecosystems, four bets, no crown: Ryobi wins the value-and-variety life, DeWalt wins the buy-anywhere broad middle with dual-voltage headroom, Milwaukee wins the daily-use premium tier, and Makita wins the refinement-first deliberate buyer. Find yourself in that sentence, buy that system's combo kit, and exit the forum wars — the batteries you're about to accumulate will make the decision permanent anyway, so make it about your work, not the argument.
