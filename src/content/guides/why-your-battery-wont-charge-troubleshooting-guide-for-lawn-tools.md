It's the first warm Saturday of spring. You pull the mower battery off the garage shelf where it spent the winter, drop it on the charger, and get... a blinking red light. The manual, if you can find it, says that means "defective." Here's the thing the blinking light doesn't tell you: a huge share of lawn tool batteries flagged as defective every spring are nothing of the sort. They're cold, or they're deeply discharged into a protective sleep, or their contacts are dirty — all recoverable, all common, and all diagnosable in your kitchen in under an hour. Let's read what the charger is actually saying.

## What the blinking light means (it's not one thing)

Chargers talk in light patterns, and the patterns mean different things — sometimes opposite things — across brands. Broadly, there are three messages:

**"Charging normally."** On several platforms a flashing light IS the charging indicator — on many DeWalt chargers, for example, a blinking red light means the pack is charging and turns solid when full. Plenty of "broken" batteries are just batteries whose owner expected solid and saw blinking. Check your charger's label before anything else.

**"Temperature hold."** A distinct pattern — red-green alternating on many Makita chargers, a long-flash/short-flash on some Black+Decker units, all-LEDs-flashing on others — means the pack is too hot or too cold to charge safely, and the charger is waiting. Lithium-ion packs generally accept a charge only within roughly 40–104°F, and the charger enforces it.

**"Fault."** A rapid or erratic flash — rapid red on Makita and several others, alternating red/green on Ryobi-style chargers — means the charger's electronics measured something out of spec inside the pack: a cell voltage too low, too high, or an open circuit. This is the "defective" verdict. Sometimes it's right. Often, with an over-wintered pack, it's a deeply discharged battery the charger refuses to touch — a recoverable condition we'll get to.

## The kitchen-table diagnostic

Work this in order; each step is a couple of minutes.

**1. Warm it up.** If the battery lived in a cold garage, bring it — and ideally the charger — indoors and wait two hours. Not on a heater, not in the oven, just room temperature. Cold is the single most common cause of spring charging refusals, and it fixes itself with patience. A pack below freezing shouldn't be charged at all; charging below about 32°F can permanently damage lithium cells even when it appears to work.

**2. Clean and reseat.** Pull the pack, look at both sets of contacts. Grass dust, oxide film and cobwebs all add resistance. Dry cloth first; isopropyl alcohol on a swab for the stubborn film; fully dry before reconnecting. Then seat the pack gently and completely — one widely-shared discovery among frustrated owners is that slamming a pack onto a charger can bounce the contacts, while a slow, deliberate seat gets a green light.

**3. Blame the charger, not just the battery.** Test the outlet with a lamp. If you have a second pack, try it on this charger; if you have a second charger, try this pack on it. Chargers fail more often than people assume — a charger that shows no light on any battery, on a known-live outlet, is your culprit, and replacing a charger is far cheaper than replacing packs one by one while the real problem laughs.

**4. Check the fuel gauge.** Most packs have an onboard button and LED gauge. Press it. Any lights at all means the pack has usable voltage and the problem is more likely temperature, contacts or charger. No lights at all points to deep discharge — keep reading.

## The deep-sleep problem: why stored batteries "die"

Lithium-ion packs self-discharge slowly all winter, and the battery management system (BMS) inside the pack sips a little power on top of that. Store a pack half-empty in October, and by April its cell voltage can drop below the floor the BMS considers safe. At that point the BMS locks the pack down — a protective deep sleep — and here's the trap: the charger sees a voltage so low it reads as a damaged cell, flashes its fault pattern, and refuses to charge. The pack isn't dead. The pack is protecting itself, and the charger is being cautious.

Two legitimate recovery moves exist. First, the pulse method: put the pack on the charger for a few seconds, remove it, repeat several times. Short exposure often nudges enough charge in to lift the pack above the lockout threshold, at which point a normal charge starts. Second, some smart chargers have a low-voltage recovery mode that trickles automatically — leave the pack on for an hour and check back. If neither works, there is a third method circulating online involving jumping the pack from a healthy battery — and that one crosses a safety line. The full picture, including exactly why, lives in the [safe lithium battery revival guide](/how-to-revive-a-dead-lithium-battery-safe-methods-only/).

Two of those deserve emphasis. A pack that charges to full but collapses the moment the mower blade engages has high internal resistance from worn cells — voltage without current, the battery equivalent of an engine that idles but can't drive. No recovery method fixes worn-out chemistry; lawn tool packs typically give roughly three to five seasons depending on care. And a swollen or damaged pack isn't a candidate for anything except careful disposal: don't charge it, don't trash it, don't drop it in the retail recycling bin (those bins are for healthy batteries — tape the terminals first). Damaged packs go through your household hazardous waste program. The [battery safety guide](/battery-tool-safety-for-homeowners-essential-guide/) covers handling and disposal properly.

One more modern wrinkle: warranty. Lawn tool batteries frequently carry multi-year coverage — some outdoor platforms warranty batteries for three to five years — and a genuine cell fault inside that window is a free replacement. People jump-start hundred-dollar packs that the manufacturer would have swapped for nothing. Check the paperwork before the pliers.

## Quick answers

**Why is my battery flashing red and green on the charger?** On most platforms that's either a temperature hold (too hot or too cold — wait it out at room temperature) or a fault verdict on a deeply discharged pack. Warm it, clean the contacts, then try the pulse method before writing it off.

**Can a completely dead lawn tool battery be saved?** Often, if "dead" means deep-discharged from storage: warm it up, pulse it on the charger, or use a charger with recovery mode. Not if the cells are worn out or the pack is physically damaged.

**How long do battery mower batteries last?** Commonly around three to five years of seasons, heavily influenced by storage habits. Half-charged indoor winter storage is the single biggest life-extender.

**Should I leave the battery on the charger all winter?** No. Charge to about half, take it off, store it indoors, and top it up once mid-winter. Living at 100% on a charger ages cells and, per fire-safety guidance, unattended charging is a risk that buys you nothing.

**The battery works in the tool but won't charge — how?** A pack can have enough charge to run a light load while sitting below the charger's acceptance threshold, or one set of contacts is dirty while the other still connects. Clean everything, seat slowly, and test on a second charger.

## The bottom line

A blinking charger is a message, not a death certificate. Warm the pack, clean the contacts, prove the charger, check the gauge — and if winter storage drained it into a protective sleep, pulse it back gently. Real death looks different: swelling, heat, persistent fault codes at room temperature, or a pack that quits under load. Those get recycled properly and replaced, ideally under the warranty you forgot the battery had.
