Somewhere in your garage right now there's probably a drill with a dead battery, a charger with a blinking light you've never decoded, and a mower you're hoping starts next spring. That's normal. Battery tools have quietly taken over the homeowner workshop — they're better than the gas and corded stuff they replaced in almost every way — but they brought a new set of skills with them, and nobody handed out the manual. How you charge, store and winter a lithium-ion pack now matters more than how you oil a hinge ever did.

This hub is that manual. It's organized the way problems actually show up: something won't work, something needs charging or storing, you're buying into a platform, or you're setting up the space itself. Each section points to a full guide.

## When something won't work

Start here, because this is why most people land on a page like this. Nine times out of ten the "broken" thing isn't broken.

If a **tool** runs rough, stops under load, or won't start at all, work through the [cordless tool troubleshooting guide](/troubleshooting-cordless-tools-homeowner-safe-fixes/) — it separates the two-minute fixes (contacts, battery seating, overheat lockouts) from the genuine failures, and it's honest about which repairs a homeowner should not attempt.

If a **battery won't charge**, the charger's blinking light is trying to tell you something specific. Most "dead" packs are actually in a temperature lockout or a protective deep-sleep, not dead. The [battery won't charge troubleshooting guide](/why-your-battery-wont-charge-troubleshooting-guide-for-lawn-tools/) decodes the light patterns brand by brand, and the [safe battery revival guide](/how-to-revive-a-dead-lithium-battery-safe-methods-only/) covers what you can legitimately do about a pack the charger refuses — and why the jump-start trick all over YouTube is the one method to skip.

## Batteries: the part that actually needs you

Here's the mental shift battery tools require: the tool is durable, the battery is perishable, and almost everything that shortens a battery's life happens while you're not using it. Left on the charger for weeks. Stored dead. Stored full. Frozen in the garage all winter. Baked on a sunny windowsill in July.

## Buying in: platforms before tools

The single most consequential decision in cordless tools isn't which drill to buy — it's which battery platform to marry. Batteries and chargers only work within one brand's system, they're where the real money goes, and switching later means abandoning that investment. ## The space itself

A workshop that fights you gets abandoned, and most homeowner workshops fail on the same three things: not enough light where the cutting happens, no dedicated home for charging so batteries end up on whatever surface was empty, and a floor plan that puts the person between the tool and the door. [How to use power tools safely in a small garage](/how-to-use-power-tools-safely-in-a-small-garage-space-lighting-and-workflow-tips/) covers the layout, lighting and workflow habits that make a one-car garage work like a shop — where the cutting happens, where the batteries live, what needs light, and the handful of habits that prevent the classic small-space accidents.

## Quick answers

**What charge level should batteries be stored at?** Around 40–50%, off the charger, somewhere that stays above freezing. Full and empty are both bad places for a lithium-ion pack to sit.

**Why won't my battery charge after winter in the garage?** Usually cold lockout (warm it indoors a couple of hours) or deep discharge from sitting — the charger reads a deeply drained pack as defective. The [won't-charge guide](/why-your-battery-wont-charge-troubleshooting-guide-for-lawn-tools/) walks the whole diagnosis.

**Are battery tools worth it over gas and corded?** For nearly all homeowner use, yes — instant start, no fuel, little maintenance. The honest exceptions are all-day heavy work and a few big-acreage yard cases, covered in the [mower comparison](/battery-mower-vs-gas-mower-which-is-better-for-homeowners/).

**Do I really have to stay on one battery brand?** Within a system, yes — packs aren't cross-compatible, and adapter workarounds void warranties. That's why the [platform decision](/how-to-choose-the-right-battery-platform-homeowner-guide/) comes before the first tool.

**Where do old tool batteries go?** Free drop-off bins at major hardware retailers handle healthy packs (tape the terminals). Swollen or damaged packs are different — those go through household hazardous waste. Details in the [safety guide](/battery-tool-safety-for-homeowners-essential-guide/).

## The bottom line

Battery tools rewrote the homeowner workshop, and the skills that matter now are battery skills: charge at room temperature, store at half-charge, keep packs out of the heat and cold, decode the blinking light before declaring anything dead, and treat a swollen pack as the hazard it is. Every guide linked above goes deep on one piece of that. Pick the one that matches today's problem — there's always one.
