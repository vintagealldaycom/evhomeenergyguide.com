There are two kinds of battery lawn tool owners in April. The first kind clicks a charged pack into the mower and cuts grass. The second kind stands at a charger watching a blinking fault light, googling why a battery that worked perfectly in October is now "defective." The difference between them isn't luck or brand — it's about ninety minutes of October work, most of which is really about where five pounds of lithium spends the winter. Battery gear actually made winterizing dramatically easier than the gas era (no fuel to stabilize, no carburetors to drain, no oil to change), but it swapped those chores for exactly one non-negotiable: get the batteries right. Here's the complete shutdown, in the order to do it.

## Step one: the batteries (do this even if you skip everything else)

The whole winter problem in one paragraph: lithium packs self-discharge slowly all winter, their internal electronics sip a little besides, and a pack that starts winter low can drift below the protective cutoff where its management system locks it out — at which point the spring charger reads it as damaged and refuses it. Meanwhile, packs stored in freezing garages and sheds lose real capacity to the cold itself, and packs stored at 100% age their cells all winter at the other extreme. Every spring "dead battery" story is one of those three plots.

So the ritual: run or charge every pack to roughly **40–50%** — two bars of four on most fuel gauges. Pull every pack from its tool, including the spare in the mower's storage slot everyone forgets. Bring them **indoors** — basement shelf, utility closet, anywhere that stays roughly people-temperature (40–70°F), away from sunlight, heaters and anything flammable, terminals facing up or covered. Households with a real fleet should give them the [ventilated battery cabinet](/how-to-build-a-fire-resistant-battery-storage-cabinet/) treatment. And chargers get unplugged for the season — nothing winters on a charger.

Then set one calendar reminder for mid-January: press each pack's gauge button, and top anything that's dropped to one bar back up to half (warm packs charge happily indoors; a pack straight from anywhere cold waits two hours first). That single check is the difference-maker for long winters — it catches the slow drifters before they cross the lockout line. If a pack does come out of winter refusing to charge anyway, the [safe revival guide](/how-to-revive-a-dead-lithium-battery-safe-methods-only/) is the gentle path back.

## Step two: the mower

Battery out (already done). Now put the mower away clean and dry:

**Deck:** scrape the underside completely — dried clippings hold moisture against steel all winter, and winter is when that becomes rust. After the scrape, a light coat of silicone spray on the clean deck makes it both rust-resistant and nonstick for spring.

**Blade decision:** this is the smart moment to sharpen or replace, not April. The blade comes off anyway for a proper deck cleaning; sharpening it now (or dropping it at the hardware store during their empty season) means spring day one is a mowing day. Mark the blade's orientation before removal, and reinstall to the manual's torque.

**Body and vents:** brush the motor vents and battery bay clear, wipe the housing, check the bag or mulch plug for holes and wash the bag if it's filthy (fully dry before storage — a damp bag molds by February).

**Parking:** somewhere dry, off bare concrete if you can manage a scrap of plywood underneath, folded or vertical if the mower supports it — one of battery mowers' quiet gifts is that most fold and store upright with no fuel or oil to leak, taking a third of the floor space. Cover it against dust, not with plastic film that traps moisture; a breathable cover or old sheet is right.

## Step three: trimmer, blower and the small gear

Fifteen minutes covers the whole supporting cast. Trimmer: strip the wrapped grass off the head, and pull the line off the spool or at least expect to respool in spring — line left loaded through winter goes brittle and shatters in use (a soak before spring use helps old line, but fresh line is cheap). Wipe the shaft, check the guard's screws. Blower: clear the intake screen and impeller area of leaf fragments, wipe it down. Hedge trimmer if you have one: brush the blade clean, wipe the bar with light oil against rust. Everything hangs or shelves **dry and off the floor** — wall hooks are ideal, both for moisture and for keeping handles from taking a winter of floor knocks.

## What about gear that must winter in the cold?

Sometimes there's no indoor option — a cabin, a detached shed, a no-basement house. Triage in this order: the batteries are the only part that truly cannot winter frozen, and they're also the smallest — even a kitchen cabinet indoors beats the best shed arrangement, so move the packs and let the tools take the cold, which they handle fine. If even that fails, an insulated cooler or foam-lined box in the shed buffers the temperature swings — genuine harm reduction, not a solution; expect some capacity cost from a hard-freeze winter regardless. What's never acceptable: packs stored empty in the cold (that's the guaranteed spring lockout) or charging a still-cold pack in spring, which causes permanent damage — cold packs warm indoors for a couple of hours first, always.

## Spring reversal, briefly

The whole restart is the shutdown in reverse and takes ten minutes: packs warm (they're already indoors, so they are), charge fully, reinstall the sharpened blade if it's off, respool the trimmer, click in a battery, go. No fuel, no primer, no prayer. If anything balks, it's nearly always a pack that missed the January check — the [won't-charge diagnostic](/why-your-battery-wont-charge-troubleshooting-guide-for-lawn-tools/) sorts it out. And the in-season habits that keep everything strong until next October live in the [lawn tool maintenance guide](/how-to-maintain-battery-lawn-tools-homeowner-guide/).

## Quick answers

**What charge should lawn tool batteries be stored at over winter?** About 40–50% — two of four bars. Not full (ages the cells), never empty (risks permanent lockout). Top back to half at a January check.

**Can batteries stay in the garage over winter?** Not in a garage that freezes. Cold storage costs real capacity and sets up charging problems; packs winter indoors at room temperature. The tools themselves can stay in the cold happily.

**Do battery mowers need winterizing like gas mowers?** Much less — no fuel, oil or carburetor work. Winterizing is: battery out and indoors at half charge, deck cleaned, blade sharpened, stored dry. Ninety minutes total.

**Should I leave the battery on the charger all winter so it's ready?** No — that parks it at 100% for months, aging it, and violates the basic rule against long unattended charger time. Half-charge on an indoor shelf, charge fully in spring.

**Why won't my mower battery charge after winter?** Usually it's cold (warm it indoors two hours) or it drifted below the protective cutoff during storage and the charger reads it as faulty. Warm, clean the contacts, and try pulse charging before declaring it dead.

## The bottom line

Winter storage for battery lawn gear is one command with decorations: get the lithium indoors at half charge. Around that center, scrape the deck while it's easy, sharpen the blade while the shops are empty, strip the trimmer line, store everything dry, and spend five minutes in January pressing gauge buttons. Do the ninety-minute October version and April belongs to the first kind of owner — the one who's mowing while the neighbor googles blinking lights.
