A common worry before installing a home charger is that it will make the electric bill balloon, and the reassuring reality is that a Level 2 charger adds a predictable, moderate amount, typically 25 to 35 percent to a household's electricity use for average driving, or roughly 40 to 70 dollars a month. A Level 2 charger is a large appliance, but it only costs money while it is actually charging, and how much it draws follows simple electrical math. This guide explains how much power a Level 2 charger uses, what it adds to your bill, why the wall always draws a bit more than reaches the battery, and the basic units, kW, kWh, amps, and volts, that make it all make sense.

The one distinction worth holding onto is power versus energy. Power, in kilowatts, is the rate a charger draws electricity at any instant; energy, in kilowatt-hours, is power accumulated over time, and energy is what your utility bills. A Level 2 charger draws high power, commonly 7 to 11 kW, but only while charging, so what hits your bill is the energy, kW times hours. This is the same kWh that drives every figure in our [cost-to-charge guide](/how-much-does-it-cost-to-charge-an-ev-at-home-2026-guide/).

## How much power a Level 2 charger draws

Level 2 charging runs on a 240-volt circuit, the same voltage as an electric dryer or oven, and the current depends on the charger and circuit, typically 32 to 48 amps. Multiplying volts by amps gives power: a 240-volt, 40-amp setup delivers about 9.6 kW, and a 48-amp unit about 11.5 kW. At those rates a charger adds roughly 25 to 40 miles of range per hour, which is why Level 2 is the practical choice for home charging over the much slower Level 1. The draw is substantial while active, but it runs only for the hours needed to replace your driving, usually a few hours overnight.

Despite the high power draw, the monthly bill impact is moderate because you only pay for energy actually used. For average driving, a Level 2 charger raises a household's electricity use by roughly 25 to 35 percent, which lands near 40 to 70 dollars a month at national-average rates. Charging off-peak cuts that substantially, as our [time-of-use guide](/smart-charging-time-of-use-tou-2026-home-charging-optimization-guide/) shows. Importantly, Level 1 and Level 2 cost the same per kWh, since the electricity comes from the same meter; Level 2 is just faster, and slightly more efficient, not more expensive per unit of energy.

## Why the wall draws more than the battery

  <div class="callout info"><span class="co-icon"></span><div><strong>Charging is about 90 percent efficient on Level 2.</strong> Some energy becomes heat in the charger, the car's onboard converter, and the battery system. Here is a subtlety that surprises careful budgeters: your car reports the kWh it added to the battery, but your utility bills the kWh pulled from the wall, and the two differ by about ten percent on a Level 2 charger. That gap is charging loss, energy converted to heat in the charger electronics, the onboard converter, and the battery management system. It is why the honest cost formula divides battery energy by efficiency, and why your bill runs slightly above a calculation based on the car's numbers, a point our [higher-than-expected guide](/why-your-ev-charging-cost-is-higher-than-expected-2026-guide/) also raises. Level 1 charging is a bit less efficient, near 85 percent.

## Putting it together

Once the units click, the whole picture is simple: a Level 2 charger is a high-power, 240-volt appliance that draws 7 to 11 kW while active, runs a few hours a night, and adds a predictable 40 to 70 dollars a month for average driving, less off-peak. It costs the same per kWh as slow charging but finishes far faster, and the wall draws about ten percent more energy than the battery keeps. There is nothing mysterious about the bill impact; it is your driving, converted to kWh, times your rate, which the [Sim-Lab](/en/sim-lab/charging-cost/) will compute from your own numbers.

## Level 1 versus Level 2 on the bill

Since many new owners wonder whether the faster charger will cost more to run, it is worth settling directly: Level 1 and Level 2 charging cost essentially the same per kWh, because both pull from the same residential meter at the same rate. The difference is speed and a small efficiency edge, not price per unit of energy. Level 1, on a standard 120-volt outlet, trickles in a few miles of range per hour and is slightly less efficient at around 85 percent, so it wastes marginally more energy as heat. Level 2, on a 240-volt circuit, adds range several times faster and runs nearer 90 percent efficiency, so over time it actually costs a hair less for the same delivered energy. The reason to choose Level 2 is therefore convenience: it replaces a day's driving in a couple of overnight hours instead of struggling to keep up. The bill impact is set by how much you drive, not by which level you use, so the faster charger does not carry a running-cost penalty, only the one-time installation.

## Quick answers

**How much power does a Level 2 charger use?** Commonly 7 to 11 kW while charging, from a 240-volt circuit at 32 to 48 amps. Only while actively charging.

**What does it add to my bill?** Roughly 40 to 70 dollars a month for average driving at average rates, less on an off-peak plan.

**Is Level 2 more expensive per mile than Level 1?** No. Same rate per kWh from the same meter. Level 2 is just faster and slightly more efficient.

**Why is my bill higher than the car's kWh figure?** Charging losses. The wall draws about ten percent more than the battery stores; your utility bills the wall.

## The bottom line

A home Level 2 charger draws high power, 7 to 11 kW, but only while charging, so it adds a moderate and predictable 40 to 70 dollars a month for average driving, roughly a 25 to 35 percent bump to a household's electricity use, and less on an off-peak plan. It costs the same per kWh as slow charging, just faster, and the wall draws about ten percent more energy than reaches the battery. Understand kW versus kWh and the bill holds no surprises. The one-time installation aside, a Level 2 charger is simply a faster way to buy the same cheap energy, and its monthly footprint on your bill is modest, predictable, and easy to shrink further by charging overnight.
