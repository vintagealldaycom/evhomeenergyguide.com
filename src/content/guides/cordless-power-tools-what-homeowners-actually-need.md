Walk the tool aisle and the message is that a proper homeowner owns forty cordless tools, each solving a problem you didn't know you had. Meanwhile the actual data of home life — the shelf that needs hanging, the toilet seat that needs swapping, the deck board that popped — gets handled by about four tools doing 95% of the work. This guide is the anti-catalog: what you genuinely need, ranked by how life actually generates tasks, plus the honest tier of "buy it when the project demands it" and the small hall of fame of purchases people regret. No "best" lists, no model shootouts — just which tools earn a spot, and why. (One decision does come before any of this: which battery platform you're buying into, because that choice outlives every individual tool. That's the [platform guide](/how-to-choose-the-right-battery-platform-homeowner-guide/) — read it first if you're starting from zero.)

## Tier one: the tools that do almost everything

**A drill/driver.** The obvious one, and still underrated. Drilling holes and driving screws covers the overwhelming majority of household tasks, and a mid-range brushless drill from any major platform is more tool than most homes will ever exhaust. If you buy one cordless tool, it's this — usually in a kit with two batteries and the charger, which is the cheapest way batteries ever get bought.

**An impact driver.** The tool people don't know they need until they use one, then can't give back. It drives screws and lags with rotational hammering that makes long fasteners feel effortless, doesn't cam out and strip heads the way drills do, and it's smaller and lighter than the drill it mostly replaces for fastening. Drill for holes, impact for screws — the two-tool combo is the real foundation, and combo kits price it accordingly.

**A cordless vacuum (shop style).** Unsexy, constantly used. Drywall dust, the car, the workbench, the mystery pile — a hand or compact shop vac on your platform gets grabbed weekly forever.

**Work light.** Nearly free as a bare tool, runs on the packs you already own, and transforms every under-sink, attic and breaker-panel job. The highest usefulness-per-dollar object in the whole ecosystem.

The discipline for this tier is simple and saves the most money: buy the bare tool (no battery) the week a real project demands it, running it on the packs you already own. That's the entire economic advantage of committing to one platform — tier-two tools cost roughly half as much bare, and your batteries already exist. What breaks people is buying tier two speculatively: the jigsaw purchased for someday sits in its box while its warranty quietly expires.

Outdoor power gear — mower, trimmer, blower — is its own parallel track with its own logic and often its own higher-voltage platform; the [battery vs gas mower comparison](/battery-mower-vs-gas-mower-which-is-better-for-homeowners/) is the entry point to that decision, and it's genuinely separate from the shop-tool question.

## The regret list

Every tool wall has one or two of these, purchased with love, used twice:

**The 40-tool mega kit.** The bundle math looks unbeatable until you inventory what you'll use: four tools constantly, six occasionally, thirty never. Kits also lock every slot to that brand's budget line. Buy the two-tool combo plus bare tools as needed; it wins on both money and quality.

**The specialty tool for one job.** The cordless caulk gun, the power scrubber, the inflator you bought instead of checking if your compressor... fine tools, real uses — for people with recurring versions of those jobs. For a one-time task, borrow, rent, or do it manually.

**Duplicate capability in a second platform.** The sale-priced drill in a different color than your batteries is not a deal; it's a second charger, incompatible packs, and a permanent tax. Platform discipline is boring and correct.

**Underpowered "compact" everything.** Compact tools are wonderful as a deliberate choice for light work and tight spaces. As the accidental default — because the kit was cheapest — they generate the "cordless tools are weak" myth one strained cut at a time. Match the tool class (and the battery size; a big saw on a tiny pack starves) to the actual work.

## Buying sense that isn't a model roundup

A few durable rules that survive every product cycle. Brushless is worth it on anything you'll use regularly — cooler running, longer tool life, better runtime — while brushed is fine for the rarely-used. Batteries are the real spend over time, so kit purchases and battery-included promos are where the money math happens; tools are almost the accessory. Two batteries is the working minimum for any tool you rely on — one working, one charged — and how you treat those packs (charging habits, half-charge storage) determines the cost of ownership more than any purchase decision, which is why the [charging guide](/how-to-charge-cordless-tool-batteries-safely/) and [storage guide](/how-to-store-cordless-power-tools-safely-battery-first-approach/) are effectively part of the buying process. And warranty registration, the chore everyone skips: major brands cover tools for years, and the registration you didn't do is the free replacement you didn't get.

## Quick answers

**What's the first cordless tool a homeowner should buy?** A drill/driver — ideally as a drill-plus-impact-driver combo kit with two batteries, because that pairing covers the vast majority of household tasks and prices the batteries efficiently.

**Do I really need both a drill and an impact driver?** Need is strong, but it's the upgrade with the highest payoff in the ecosystem: the impact drives fasteners dramatically better, and the drill stays free for holes. Combo kits exist because this pairing is the right answer for most people.

**Are big cordless tool kits worth it?** Rarely. You pay for tools you'll never use, all locked to one brand's budget tier. The combo-plus-bare-tools path is cheaper over five years and yields better tools.

**Is brushless worth the extra money?** For your frequently used tools, yes — more power, less heat, longer life. For the once-a-year tool, brushed is fine and the savings are real.

**How many batteries do I need?** Two per platform minimum, sized to your biggest tool's appetite. Beyond that, add packs only when swapping mid-job becomes a real annoyance — and store the fleet at half-charge.

## The bottom line

The honest homeowner kit is four tools deep — drill, impact driver, vacuum, light — with a bench of project-triggered bare-tool additions behind it and a hard rule against second platforms and speculative purchases. Buy the combo kit, register the warranty, treat the batteries right, and let real projects, not the tool aisle, decide what joins the wall next. Your money ends up in tools that get used, which is the only tool-buying metric that matters.
