In a one-car garage, the charger goes near the car and you're done. A two-car garage turns mounting into a small logistics puzzle: two parking bays, two possible vehicles (whose charge ports may sit on opposite corners), one charger, and a cable of finite length. Get the placement right and charging is effortless for any car in either bay; get it wrong and someone's doing a cable stretch or a bay swap every week for years. Here's the logic that gets it right the first time.

## Start with the ports, not the walls

The instinct is to pick a convenient wall and mount there. The correct starting point is the opposite: **map where the charge ports actually end up when the cars park.** Charge ports live all over — front fenders, rear quarters, left side, right side, nose — and the port's position when parked, not the car's position, is what the cable must reach.

## The placements that usually win

Across the common cases, three mounting strategies cover most two-car garages:

**The center pillar or back-center wall.** If your garage has a structural point between the bays — a pillar between doors, or the back wall's midpoint — a charger there sits within reach of both bays symmetrically. With a typical long cable, a center-mounted unit reaches ports on the near side of either car easily and the far side of either car acceptably. This is the classic best answer when the wiring can reach it: maximum flexibility, no favoritism between bays.

**The between-cars side position.** Mounting on a side wall, positioned toward the *front* of the bays (since cars usually nose in and many ports sit forward), reaches the adjacent car trivially and the far car across the front of both vehicles. It works best when the frequent charger — the daily commuter — parks in the adjacent bay, and the far bay's car charges less often. If your household has a clear primary-charger car, giving it the near bay simplifies everything.

**The rear-wall position for nose-out parkers.** Households that back in reverse the geometry: ports that face the rear wall want the charger there. The mapping exercise catches this automatically — which is the point of doing it rather than defaulting to a wall.

That last smell deserves a word: bay-swap plans fail the way all [human-dependent arrangements](/blog/charging-two-evs-on-one-circuit/) fail — reliably, at 11 p.m., in the rain. Placement that works with the cars where they naturally land is worth a longer cable or a smarter mounting spot.

## Cable length is half the placement decision

The charger's cable length is a spec you choose at purchase, and in a two-car garage it's as important as the mounting point — the [full cable-length guide](/blog/charger-cable-length-driveway/) covers the details, but the two-car essentials are: measure the *worst-case* path (mounting point → around/over the far car → its far-corner port), remember the cable travels in drapes and arcs rather than straight lines (add meaningful margin over tape-measure distance), and favor the longer option when torn — extra length hangs neatly on a hook; missing length is a permanent daily annoyance. A generous cable also buys future-proofing against the next car's port surprising you.

Mind the height, too: mounting the unit around chest height with a cable hook beside it keeps the cable's home position off the floor, out from underfoot, and clear of [being driven over](/blog/charging-cable-feels-warm/) — the garage cable's classic slow killer.

## Wiring, circuits, and the second-charger future

Placement interacts with the electrical work in two ways worth planning together. First, the mounting spot has to be reachable by the circuit at reasonable cost — a perfect center-pillar position that requires a long conduit odyssey [changes the quote](/blog/charger-distance-from-panel/); usually there's a placement that satisfies both the cable map and the wiring path. Second, if a second EV is in the household's future, this is the moment to think one step ahead: wiring the chosen location with capacity for [two load-sharing chargers](/blog/charging-two-evs-on-one-circuit/) later — or at least routing conduit with room for a second run — costs little now and saves an entire project later. A two-car garage with two EVs eventually wants a connector at each bay; placing today's charger as one of that future pair (rather than dead center) is a legitimate strategy if the second EV is more "when" than "if."

## The dry-run test before anyone drills

The cheapest insurance in this whole decision costs one evening: tape a cardboard square to the candidate wall spot, take a rope cut to your candidate cable length, and physically walk the connection to both cars in their natural positions — including the annoying version, where the far car parked six inches lazier than usual. Five minutes of pantomime reveals what diagrams hide: the cable that crosses a door path at shin height, the drape that lands on a hood, the stretch that only works when someone parks carefully. Households that run the dry-run almost never join the placement-regret club; households that mount by eye contribute most of its members. Do the pantomime, adjust the tape, *then* call the electrician — walls forgive tape, not conduit.

## Quick answers

**Should the charger favor the EV's bay or sit centered?** One EV + one gas car: favor the EV's bay, biased toward center for the future. Two EVs (or soon): center, or plan the two-charger layout now.

**What if my garage door tracks block the ideal wall spot?** Mounting between or below track hardware is common — installers navigate this daily. Ceiling-drop cable reels are the exotic-but-real solution for truly blocked walls.

**Can the charger live outside the garage and serve cars inside?** Through-the-wall placements (unit outside, or cable passing through) exist but complicate weatherproofing and code — usually a sign the indoor mapping deserves another look.

**Does placement affect charging speed?** Not at all — placement is purely about reach and daily convenience. Speed lives in the [circuit and the car](/blog/do-all-evs-charge-same-speed/).

## The bottom line

Two-car garage placement is a mapping exercise, not a wall-picking exercise: mark where the ports actually land, find the mounting point that reaches both marks with honest cable slack, keep the cable off the floor and out of walkways, and let the wiring path negotiate with — not dictate — the answer. An hour with tape and a tape measure before the electrician arrives buys years of charging that just works, whichever car pulls in first.
