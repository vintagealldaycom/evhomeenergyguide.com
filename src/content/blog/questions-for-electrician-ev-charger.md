An EV charger installation is, for most homeowners, the biggest electrical project since the house was built — and the conversation with the electrician is where its quality gets decided. You don't need to speak fluent electrician; you need the right dozen questions, asked before work starts. Here they are, organized by what each one protects, with the answers you should expect to hear.

## The capacity questions (protecting your whole house)

**"Will you do a load calculation, and does my panel have capacity for this?"** This is the foundational question. A charger adds a large continuous load, and the correct first step is a formal load calculation against your panel's rating — not a glance at empty breaker slots. The answer you want: "yes, I'll run the calculation" — followed by a number. If your panel is tight, the conversation branches honestly into options (see below) rather than proceeding on hope. An installer who waves this off is telling you something.

**"If capacity is tight, what are my options besides a full panel upgrade?"** The modern answer includes more than the expensive default: [load-management devices](/blog/what-makes-a-charger-smart/) that pause charging when the house peaks, a lower [amperage tier](/blog/how-to-choose-between-32a-40a-48a/) that fits the available headroom, or circuit-sharing arrangements. A good electrician lays out the ladder; a great one tells you which rung your actual usage justifies.

## The sizing questions (protecting your money)

**"What amperage do you recommend for my car and driving — and why?"** The answer should reference *your* situation: your car's [onboard charger rating](/blog/onboard-charger-explained/), your daily miles, your panel's headroom. Be alert for reflexive maximum-sizing — "everyone gets 48A" is only right when your car and panel say so. Overnight hours make [40A a comfortable default](/blog/how-to-choose-between-32a-40a-48a/) for most; let the reasoning, not the reflex, pick the number.

**"Plug-in receptacle or hardwired — and which do you recommend here?"** Both are legitimate; [the trade-offs are real](/blog/nema-14-50-vs-hardwired-charger/) (flexibility vs. maximum amperage, GFCI-breaker differences, receptacle quality stakes). What you're listening for is a recommendation *with reasons tied to your setup* — and, if a receptacle, an unprompted mention of using an industrial/EV-rated one, which is the tell of an installer who's seen the [melted-outlet failure mode](/blog/melted-or-discolored-plug/).

**"What wire gauge for my run length, and what's the voltage drop?"** For any longer run, the [distance math](/blog/charger-distance-from-panel/) should visibly enter the quote. You don't need to check their arithmetic — you need to hear that the arithmetic happened.

## The paperwork questions (protecting your coverage)

**"Will you pull the permit, and is the inspection included in the quote?"** The right answer is an untroubled yes — [permits are routine](/blog/ev-charger-installation-permit/) for professionals, and hesitation here is the single loudest red flag in the whole conversation. Unpermitted work saves a small fee and risks your insurance coverage and resale cleanliness.

**"Are you licensed and insured for this work in this jurisdiction — and can rebate paperwork use your license number?"** Matter-of-fact for a legitimate pro. The rebate half is practical: many [utility incentive programs](/blog/ev-charger-installation-permit/) require the installer's license details, and asking up front avoids chasing paperwork later.

## The future questions (protecting your next decade)

**"What would it cost to size the circuit for more than today's charger?"** The wire in the wall is the expensive, disruptive part; capacity above today's need is cheap while the walls are open. If a [second EV](/blog/charging-two-evs-on-one-circuit/), a faster future car, or a garage workshop is plausible, ask for the incremental price of the bigger circuit or a conduit path with room for a second run — it's often a modest add that deletes a future project.

**"Where exactly will the charger mount, and does that spot work for the cable and the cars?"** Bring your [placement homework](/blog/charger-placement-two-car-garage/) — the port map, the cable-reach measurements — and reconcile it with their preferred wiring path before anyone drills. The best install of the wrong location is still the wrong location.

## The quote questions (protecting against surprises)

**"What's included, what's excluded, and what would change the price mid-job?"** The honest answers name the usual suspects: drywall repair (often excluded), unexpected panel conditions, longer-than-estimated routing. Hearing the contingencies up front converts them from surprises into line items.

**"Have you installed EV chargers before — roughly how many?"** Routine experience shows up as unprompted mentions of continuous-load sizing, GFCI nuance, and receptacle quality. This project is standard work, but EV-specific experience smooths the details.

## After the install: the two-minute acceptance check

The questions don't quite end when the work does. Before the electrician leaves, run a short acceptance check together: a full-rate charging session started while they're present (confirming the [expected kW is actually delivered](/blog/charger-kw-rating-explained/) and nothing trips), a look at the labeled breaker in the panel, the permit/inspection status confirmed in writing, and — on plug-in installs — a first [warmth check on the receptacle](/blog/charger-plug-or-outlet-feels-hot/) twenty minutes into the session. Ask for the load calculation and any voltage-drop figures for your records; they're gold at resale and for any future electrical planning. Two minutes of verification while the ladder's still out beats a callback visit every time — and a professional who welcomes the acceptance check is confirming everything the interview questions were designed to find.

One meta-question ties the whole interview together, worth asking yourself as much as them: did the electrician ask *you* questions? A pro who wants your car's specs, your daily miles, your future-vehicle plans, and a look at your panel before quoting is running the same checklist from the other side — and that curiosity is the single most reliable predictor of an install that fits your actual life rather than a template. The best answer to "what should I ask an electrician" turns out to be: hire the one who was already asking.

## Quick answers

**How many quotes should I get?** Two or three for a full circuit install — the spread is often large, and the questions above let you compare *reasoning*, not just prices.

**Should I buy the charger first or let the electrician supply it?** Either works; buying your own [chosen model](/blog/what-makes-a-charger-smart/) first lets the install be quoted around its exact requirements (hardwire vs plug, amperage, mounting).

**Is the cheapest quote a trap?** Not inherently — but a quote that's cheapest *because* it skips the permit, the load calc, or receptacle quality is buying you the expensive kind of savings.

**What should I have ready for the visit?** Your car's onboard-charger spec, your daily miles, photos of your panel (door open, breaker labels visible), and your placement map. Ten minutes of prep makes every answer sharper.

## The bottom line

You're not auditing the electrician's craft — you're verifying the process around it: a real load calculation, sizing reasoned from your car and driving, a permit pulled without flinching, quality components at the connection points, and a circuit sized with one eye on your next decade. Ask the dozen questions, listen for reasons rather than reflexes, and the install you get will be the one your house deserves.
