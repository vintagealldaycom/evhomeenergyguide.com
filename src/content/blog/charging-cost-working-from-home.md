Working from home changes your EV math twice: you drive far less than the averages assume, and your car sits in the driveway all day, tempting you to charge whenever. The first change makes your charging bill smaller than every online estimate; the second can quietly make it bigger than it needs to be. Here's the real arithmetic for the WFH household, and the one habit that keeps you on the cheap side of it.

## Your mileage isn't the average — so your bill isn't either

Most charging-cost estimates assume the [average ~37 daily miles](/) of a commuting driver. Work from home and your reality is different: school runs, errands, gym, weekend trips — commonly 15–25 miles on a typical day, sometimes near zero. Since charging cost scales directly with miles, your bill scales down with them:

That's the headline for anyone worried an EV will blow up a WFH household budget: at 600 miles a month, you're looking at roughly a dollar a day of electricity. The car in the driveway costs less to fuel than the coffee habit.

## The WFH trap: daytime charging on the wrong plan

Now the second change. A commuter's car is away all day, so it naturally charges at night. A WFH car sits plugged-in-able at 2 p.m. — and if you're on a **time-of-use plan**, casually topping up through afternoon and early-evening hours means paying peak or shoulder rates for energy that overnight hours would sell you at half price.

The fix costs nothing: keep the commuter's habit even though you don't commute. **Plug in whenever you like, but let a [charging schedule](/blog/how-much-you-save-overnight/) delay the actual energy flow to the off-peak window.** The car still sits at a comfortable charge level (you're driving little, remember), and the WFH flexibility that created the temptation becomes irrelevant — the schedule charges at 11 p.m. whether you're home at 2 p.m. or not.

One honest exception: if you're on a **flat rate**, daytime charging costs exactly what overnight does — charge whenever. And if you have **solar**, midday charging can actually be your cheapest option, flipping this advice entirely — see [how solar changes the math](/blog/solar-panels-ev-charging-math/).

## The whole-house effect nobody warns you about

Here's a subtlety specific to WFH households considering a TOU switch for EV savings: you're home during peak hours, *using electricity*. Air conditioning at 4 p.m., cooking at 6, the home office running all afternoon — a WFH household's non-car usage skews toward exactly the hours TOU plans price highest. So while the EV charging itself saves nicely off-peak, the plan can raise the cost of the daytime living you can't shift.

The decisive move: run your utility's **plan comparison tool** with your actual usage history before switching. It knows your real daytime consumption; generic advice doesn't. Many WFH households still come out ahead on an EV-specific TOU plan — but it's the one group that should verify rather than assume.

## Level 1 might be all you need

A WFH bonus worth naming: low daily miles plus all-day-parked equals the ideal [Level 1 candidate](/blog/trickle-charging-level-1/). At 15–25 miles a day, a standard outlet replaces your driving with hours to spare — no installation, no project, and (on a flat rate) no meaningful cost difference. Many WFH households discover the Level 2 charger they budgeted for is optional. Try the included cable for a month before spending; your driving log will tell you the truth.

## The commuter-turned-WFH transition

If you bought the EV as a commuter and *then* went remote, revisit two decisions the commute originally made for you. Your charger sizing: the [Level 2 unit](/blog/how-to-choose-between-32a-40a-48a/) sized for daily 40-mile replenishment is now luxuriously oversized — nothing wrong with that, but if you were still deciding, the calculus has changed. And your rate plan: a TOU plan chosen when the house sat empty during peak hours deserves a re-check now that you're home running the office, the AC, and lunch through those same hours — your utility's comparison tool with a recent (post-WFH) usage year tells you whether the plan still fits. Neither re-decision is urgent; both are worth ten minutes at your next bill.

## Seasonal notes for the home-all-day car

A WFH car experiences seasons differently than a commuter's. In summer, it can sit in the garage or shade instead of an office lot — less [heat soak](/blog/charging-in-extreme-heat-cost/), less recovery AC, slightly better efficiency. In winter, short-trip driving (the WFH pattern) is the least efficient kind — the battery and cabin heat up for a 4-mile errand and cool again — so your winter efficiency dip can look surprisingly steep even at low total miles. Neither changes the strategy; both explain what you'll see in the numbers. The WFH pattern's low mileage keeps every seasonal effect small in absolute dollars — a 30% winter efficiency penalty on a $20 charging month is six dollars, which is the kind of "problem" WFH EV ownership specializes in.

## The honest comparison to your old commute

One number worth writing down before you forget it: what the commute used to cost. A pre-WFH 30-mile round trip in a 28-MPG car at $3.50/gallon was roughly $80 of gasoline a month — money the remote work already deleted. The EV's $15–35 charging line isn't just small; it's small *on top of* a transportation budget that WFH already cut to a fraction. Framed that way, the WFH-plus-EV household is running near the theoretical minimum cost of car ownership's fuel line — which is worth remembering the next time a single hot-month bill looks slightly high.

## Quick answers

**Will the utility notice I got an EV?** They'll see usage rise by your charging kWh — for a WFH driver, often a modest bump that's smaller than a season's AC swing.

**Should I charge to 80% daily even driving this little?** Yes — the [80% habit](/blog/charge-to-100-or-80/) costs a light driver literally nothing, since even 50% covers your day.

**Does the car sitting parked all day use power?** A little — EVs sip a small amount parked (connectivity, monitoring). It's single-digit dollars a month, already inside the estimates above.

## The WFH bottom line

Work from home and the EV cost story gets better, not worse: fewer miles mean a smaller bill than any commuter comparison suggests, Level 1 may cover you for free, and the only real risk — casual daytime charging on a TOU plan — is erased by one scheduled setting. Do the ten-minute setup (schedule to off-peak, or confirm your flat rate makes it moot), and your charging line item settles into the $15–35/month range where it belongs — a rounding error next to what the commute you're not doing used to cost.
