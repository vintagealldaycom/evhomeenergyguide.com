Once you've decided to install a Level 2 home charger, you hit the real fork: 32, 40, or 48 amps? The marketing pushes you toward the biggest number, the price climbs with it, and the "right" answer depends on facts about your specific car and house that the salesperson doesn't know. This guide gives you the decision framework so you buy the amperage you'll actually use — not the one with the shiniest spec.

Notice the range-added numbers bunch up at the top — the jump from 32A to 40A is bigger in practice than 40A to 48A, because [most cars' onboard chargers](/blog/onboard-charger-explained/) start capping out in that zone. That clustering is your first hint that the biggest number isn't automatically the best value.

## The two facts that decide it

Before comparing chargers, get two numbers about *your* situation. Everything else follows from them.

    <span class="vz-vs">VS</span>

**Your charging speed is capped by the lower of these two, and by the charger.** A 48A charger helps only if your car accepts ~48A *and* your panel supports a 60A circuit. Miss either and you've paid for amps that sit idle.

## Does the speed difference even matter?

Here's the reality check that saves people money. Overnight charging has *hours* to work, and you only need to replace a day's driving. The [average American drives ~37 miles a day](/):

  <div class="vz-gauge">
    <div class="vz-ring ok" style="--p:20"><span>~1.5h</span></div>
    <div class="vz-gauge-text"><strong>37 miles at 32A ≈ 1.5 hours</strong>Even the smallest of these chargers replaces a typical day's driving in well under two hours. Across an 8-hour overnight window, 32A vs 48A is the difference between "done by 11pm" and "done by 10:30pm" — invisible while you sleep.</div>

</div>

For most drivers charging overnight, **32A and 48A finish the same: fully charged by morning.** The faster unit only matters if you routinely need to top up quickly during the day, drive far above average daily miles, or share one charger between two cars. Be honest about which describes you before paying for 48A.

## The future-proofing exception

The one strong reason to install bigger than your current car needs: **the circuit, not the charger.** If you're already paying an electrician to run a new line, installing a 60A circuit (even for a car that only needs 32A) costs modestly more now and saves a full re-wire later when your next EV accepts more current. In that case, run the bigger circuit and either buy a 48A charger set to [dial down](/blog/charger-amperage-setting/) to today's car, or a 40A unit now with the circuit ready for an upgrade.

<div class="callout ok"><span class="co-icon">✓</span><div><strong>The buying rule:</strong> match the charger to your car's acceptance for today's speed, and size the <em>circuit</em> as generously as your panel and budget allow for tomorrow's car. The wire is the expensive-to-change part; the charger is swappable.</div></div>

## Beyond amperage

Once you've settled the amps, remember the amperage is only one factor in the actual unit you buy. [Cable length](/blog/portable-vs-wall-mounted-charger/) for your parking spot, weatherproofing rating if it's [outdoors](/blog/charging-ev-in-rain/), smart features, build quality, and warranty all matter as much as the headline number for living with the thing for a decade. A well-built 40A unit beats a flimsy 48A one for most owners.

The 32/40/48 decision comes down to a calm comparison: what your car accepts, what your panel supports, and whether you genuinely need daytime speed. For the majority charging overnight, 40A is the comfortable default and 32A is often plenty — with the circuit sized up for the future. Save the 48A premium for the cases that actually use it.

## The overnight-math reality check

Before agonizing over 40A versus 48A, do the honest overnight math for your actual driving. Take your typical daily miles, divide by the range-per-hour each charger adds, and see how long each would take. For most drivers, the answer is sobering in a useful way: even the smallest of these chargers replaces a normal day's driving in a couple of hours, leaving the entire rest of the overnight window as unused margin. The difference between 32A and 48A only becomes real if you regularly drain a large fraction of your battery daily, need to recharge fully in a short window, or share one charger between two cars that both need substantial charging overnight. If none of those describe you — and for most single-EV households they don't — the amperage choice is far less consequential than the marketing implies, and a mid-range 40A unit is a comfortable, future-friendly default.

## Where your money is better spent

Once amperage is settled, the factors that actually affect your daily happiness with the charger have nothing to do with amps. A cable long enough to reach your car's port without stretching. Solid weatherproofing if it lives outside. A reliable brand with good warranty support, so you're not fighting [Wi-Fi dropouts](/blog/charger-wont-connect-wifi-app/) or [firmware issues](/blog/charger-firmware-update-stuck/). Clean cable management so the cord isn't puddled on the floor getting driven over. These quality-of-life factors determine whether you love or merely tolerate your charger over its decade-plus lifespan — and they're where a slightly bigger budget pays real dividends. A well-built 40A charger you enjoy using beats a cheap 48A unit that frustrates you every week. Buy the amperage your car and panel justify, then spend your remaining budget on build quality, not on chasing the biggest number.

## A note on wire and distance

One cost factor worth asking your electrician about: the run length between panel and charger interacts with amperage. Higher amperage over a long run needs thicker wire, and thick copper over distance is where install quotes climb. If your panel is far from your parking spot, the incremental cost of 48A-capable wiring over 40A grows with every foot — sometimes enough to tip the value math toward the smaller charger. Conversely, a short run makes generous sizing cheap. It's one more reason the same charger can cost very different amounts to install in different homes, and why quotes deserve a line-item look.

## Quick answers

**Is 48A ever a mistake?** Only when the panel can't truly support 60A of new continuous load — forcing it invites nuisance trips or a costly upgrade you didn't need.

**Can I start at 32A and upgrade later?** Yes — especially if the circuit was sized generously up front. That's the future-proofing pattern working as intended.

**Does a bigger charger use more electricity?** No — it charges the same energy faster. Your bill depends on [kWh, not kW](/blog/kw-vs-kwh-explained/).
