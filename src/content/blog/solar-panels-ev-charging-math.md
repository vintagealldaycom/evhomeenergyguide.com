Add solar panels to an EV household and the charging playbook you carefully learned gets rewritten. The overnight-is-cheapest rule can invert. Your "cost" of charging becomes a question about export rates rather than retail prices. And the EV, in turn, becomes one of the best things that ever happened to your solar payback. Here's the new math, honestly laid out.

## The key concept: your solar power's real value

When your panels are producing, every kWh has a fork in its road: use it in the house (or car), or export it to the grid for credit. What that credit is worth — your **export rate** — is the number that rules everything:

Under modern reduced-export policies, the logic is simple: a solar kWh exported earns you, say, $0.05 of credit; the same kWh charged into your car displaces $0.16–0.30 of grid purchase. **Self-consumption wins big — and the EV is the biggest self-consumption sponge a household can own.**

## The new cheapest window: midday

For solar households on reduced export rates, the cheapest charging isn't overnight anymore — it's **whenever your panels are producing surplus**, typically late morning through mid-afternoon:

The practical catch is obvious: midday charging requires the car to be *home at midday*. This creates two clean player profiles. **[WFH households](/blog/charging-cost-working-from-home/), retirees, and second cars** can feast on midday surplus — for them, solar turns the daytime-charging temptation into the optimal strategy. **Commuters whose car is away all day** keep the overnight playbook: charge off-peak at night, export the midday surplus for whatever credit it earns, and let the two flows offset on the bill. Solar still helps commuters — it just helps via the bill's credit line rather than via direct charging.

## Smart solar charging: the automation layer

Chasing surplus manually (watching the production app, starting charges when the sun cooperates) gets old fast. Two automation tiers solve it. **Solar-aware chargers** — several models integrate with your solar system and modulate charging to match excess production in real time, ramping the car up as clouds pass and the dishwasher cycles, so you self-consume maximum surplus without exporting or importing. It's the premium, set-and-forget version. **The schedule approximation** — a plain [scheduled charge](/blog/how-much-you-save-overnight/) through your reliable surplus hours (say 10 a.m.–3 p.m.) captures most of the benefit without special hardware; you'll import a little on cloudy days and export a little on strong ones, and the average lands close to optimal. Either beats manual, and either beats ignoring the surplus.

## What the EV does for your solar payback

The flattering math runs both directions. Solar systems pay back through the value of their production — and value hinges on self-consumption under modern export rates. An EV adds 3,000–5,000 kWh/year of *schedulable* demand you can park directly under your production curve, converting low-value exports into high-value displaced fuel. Households evaluating solar *because* of the EV should run the numbers with the EV's consumption included: the car frequently improves the system's payback period by a meaningful margin, and it justifies sizing the array a bit larger than pre-EV usage alone would. If solar is on your someday list, the EV just moved it closer.

## Sizing solar with the EV in mind

If the panels aren't installed yet, the EV belongs in the sizing conversation from day one. An EV adds roughly 3,000–5,000 kWh of annual consumption — for many homes, a 30–50% increase over pre-EV usage — and an array sized only to the historical bills will under-produce for the electrified household. The good news: adding capacity at initial installation is far cheaper per panel than expanding later (the fixed costs — design, permitting, mounting, inverter, crew — are already being paid), so the marginal panels that cover the car are the cheapest solar you'll ever buy. Tell the installer your EV's expected annual kWh (your [cost-per-mile math](/blog/how-to-estimate-cost-per-mile/) gives it to you), and have them model the system against the electrified usage, not last year's bills. A [two-EV future](/blog/two-evs-monthly-charging-cost/) doubles the point.

## The three-way optimization: solar, TOU, and the car

Most solar households also face TOU rates, and the interaction creates a pleasingly solvable puzzle. Your energy now has three prices: surplus solar (cheapest — the forgone export credit), off-peak grid (cheap), and peak grid (expensive). The optimal routine falls out naturally: car home at midday → charge on surplus first; remaining need → overnight off-peak window; peak hours → never charge, and let the house coast on solar's tail production. Solar-aware chargers automate the first rule; an ordinary [overnight schedule](/blog/how-much-you-save-overnight/) handles the second; the third is just the discipline you already have. Households running all three report the charging line on their bill approaching invisibility — a few dollars of off-peak top-up wrapped around a core of self-consumed sunshine.

## The cloudy-season reality

One expectation-setter for new solar-EV households: the beautiful midday-surplus math is seasonal. Winter production can run at a third of summer's, and in cloudy months your surplus may not cover the car at all — the household reverts to the ordinary [off-peak grid playbook](/blog/how-much-you-save-overnight/) until spring. This isn't a flaw; it's the annual rhythm. The practical implication is to keep the overnight schedule configured year-round even during surplus season (it catches the cloudy weeks automatically), and to judge the solar-charging strategy on annual numbers, not July's. Over the year, a well-matched system still routes a large share of the car's energy through the panels — it just does it unevenly, with summer carrying the load.

## Quick answers

**Should I get a battery too?** A home battery adds the option to charge the car from stored solar at night — but batteries are expensive, and the car-charging case alone rarely justifies one. Evaluate the battery on backup power and rate arbitrage; treat car-from-battery as a bonus.

**Does solar charging change anything for the car?** Nothing — the car sees ordinary AC from your [EVSE](/blog/what-is-an-evse/). All the solar logic lives upstream of the plug.

**What about the [80% habit](/blog/charge-to-100-or-80/) with surplus to burn?** Keep it — free-ish energy doesn't change battery chemistry. Use surplus for timing, not for topping past your daily limit.

**My utility still has full net metering — do I care about any of this?** Barely — under retail-rate crediting, timing is financially neutral. Enjoy it while it lasts; policies are trending toward reduced export everywhere.

## The rewritten playbook

Solar turns charging strategy into a one-question flowchart: is the car home while the panels produce? Yes → charge on your surplus, the cheapest energy you'll ever put in a tank, automated by a solar-aware charger or a midday schedule. No → keep the overnight off-peak playbook and bank the export credits. Either way, the EV and the panels are better together than apart — each one improves the economics of the other, which is why the two purchases so often arrive in the same household within a few years of each other.
