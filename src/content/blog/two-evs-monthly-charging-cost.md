The second EV decision usually hinges on infrastructure questions — [can one circuit handle both?](/blog/charging-two-evs-on-one-circuit/) — but the budget question deserves an equally clear answer: what does a fully electric two-car household actually pay per month for charging? Here's the honest math, plus the pleasant surprise that the second EV is cheaper to add than the first was.

## The combined math

Household charging cost is just each car's miles ÷ efficiency × rate, summed — with [charging losses](/blog/charging-efficiency-loss/) included. Take a representative household: a commuter sedan (1,100 miles/month at 3.6 mi/kWh) and a family crossover (700 miles/month at 3.0 mi/kWh):

So a typical fully-electric two-car household lands around **$60–100 per month** in home charging — for 1,800 miles of driving. The gas equivalent (say both cars averaging 26 MPG combined at $3.50/gallon) runs about $242/month. The two-EV household is saving roughly **$145–180 every month** on fuel alone, which is the number that belongs in the go-electric-on-the-second-car decision.

## The rate strategy at two-EV scale

Everything about [off-peak charging](/blog/how-much-you-save-overnight/) matters roughly *twice as much* with two cars. In the worked example, the schedule saves ~$37/month — $440/year — which upgrades rate optimization from "nice habit" to "meaningful money." Two-EV specifics worth noting:

**Window capacity is rarely the issue.** ~600 kWh/month is ~20 kWh/night combined — about 2 hours of one 40A circuit's output. Even a 6-hour super-off-peak window holds both cars' needs with room to spare, [staggered or load-shared](/blog/charging-two-evs-on-one-circuit/).

**EV-specific plans get more attractive.** The bigger your shiftable load, the more a deep overnight discount is worth and the easier it outweighs the peak premium on fixed household usage. Two-EV households are the plans' ideal customers — re-run the comparison tool if you skipped the switch at one car.

**Route by efficiency when convenient.** A light habit with real returns: the [efficient car](/blog/sedan-vs-suv-ev-charging-cost/) takes the long solo errands, the big one does what it's for. At two-EV mileage volumes, the habit is worth a few dollars every month for zero effort.

## Budgeting and tracking

For a clean household number, skip per-car math and read totals: your charger app's monthly kWh (or both apps' sum) × your rate = the true, loss-inclusive household figure. Watch it for one summer and one winter month — the [cold-weather swing](/blog/charging-in-extreme-heat-cost/) at two-car scale is noticeable (often +20–35% on the charging line in a hard winter month) and knowing your seasonal range prevents budget surprises. If you reimburse work mileage or split costs in the household, per-car session logs give you the split without spreadsheet archaeology.

## The bill you'll actually see

Translating the household math onto the utility bill: your total kWh rises by both cars' combined charging (roughly 600 kWh in the worked example — a visible jump), and on a TOU plan nearly all of it should appear in the off-peak or super-off-peak bucket. That bucket concentration is your monthly health check: two scheduled cars put a large, unmistakable block of usage in the discount window, and any meaningful charging kWh appearing in peak periods means one car's [schedule silently failed](/blog/how-much-you-save-overnight/) — with two cars, twice the schedules that can drift after software updates or [power outages](/blog/charging-stopped-after-power-outage/). The two-EV household's five-minute seasonal bill check pays double what a single-car household's does, because both the savings and the silent-failure stakes are doubled.

## When the second EV changes the infrastructure math

The happy default — EV #2 rides the existing setup free — has exceptions worth budgeting honestly. If both cars are high-mileage, the [one-circuit solutions](/blog/charging-two-evs-on-one-circuit/) may need upgrading to paired load-sharing chargers (a few hundred dollars plus installation) or, rarely, a second circuit (a real project, panel-dependent). If your panel was near capacity after EV #1, the load calculation for simultaneous charging may force the conversation earlier. Neither changes the operating economics — the $60–100/month figure holds regardless of how the electrons are divided — but the honest total-cost picture for a second EV includes a hardware line that ranges from $0 (scheduling on one charger) to four figures (new circuit on a tight panel), with most households landing at the cheap end.

## The one-charger-first strategy

For households going two-EV gradually, a sensible sequencing note: don't buy the second charger before living with the shared setup. Start both cars on the existing single charger with [staggered schedules](/blog/charging-two-evs-on-one-circuit/) — for many households, the plug swap happens twice a week and never annoys anyone, and the second-charger money stays in your pocket. If after a month the swapping genuinely grates, buy the paired load-sharing unit with real knowledge of your pattern. The infrastructure can always grow; it can't shrink. The same patience applies to circuit upgrades — let demonstrated overnight shortfalls, not showroom anxiety, justify the electrician.

## Quick answers

**Will two EVs strain my home's electrical service?** Usually no — [managed properly](/blog/charging-two-evs-on-one-circuit/), the combined overnight load fits typical panels. An electrician's load calc settles edge cases.

**Is it worth a second charger?** Convenience, not economics — plug-swapping is free; paired load-sharing units end the swapping. The electricity cost is identical either way.

**What about one EV + one PHEV?** The gentlest version — the [PHEV](/blog/phev-vs-ev-charging/) adds only $10–25/month of charging and can live on Level 1, barely touching the main setup.

## A note on tracking two cars without the spreadsheet

If per-car costs matter to you (mileage reimbursement, teen-driver accountability, plain curiosity), resist the manual-log urge — the data already exists in three places. Each car's app reports its energy added; the charger's session history shows wall-side totals with timestamps; and on staggered schedules, the timestamps alone attribute every session to a car. Five minutes at month-end assembling those numbers beats any logbook, and after a few months the split is so stable you'll stop checking. Households that need a hard per-car figure for taxes or reimbursement can add an energy-monitoring plug or use the charger's per-session export — but for most, the apps' built-in totals answer the question for free.

## The bottom line

A two-EV household typically pays $60–100/month for all its driving energy at home — versus roughly $240+ for the same miles on gas. The second EV inherits paid-for infrastructure, an optimized rate, and practiced habits, making it the easiest EV adoption decision most households ever face: the savings double while the setup costs stay behind at car #1.
