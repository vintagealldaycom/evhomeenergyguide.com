Physics doesn't care about body styles, but it does care about weight and aerodynamics — and that's why an electric SUV or truck costs more per mile to charge than an electric sedan. The question is how much more, whether it should influence what you buy, and how the gap compares to the same choice in gas vehicles. Here are the honest numbers.

## Efficiency is the whole story

Charging cost per mile is [your rate divided by your car's efficiency](/blog/how-to-estimate-cost-per-mile/) — so the sedan/SUV gap is purely an efficiency gap. Real-world figures cluster predictably by size:

So the honest answer: a big electric truck can cost roughly **75% more to charge** than an efficient sedan — real money, on the order of $10–40 a month depending on how far up the size ladder you go and how much you drive.

## The context that softens it

Before the number scares you off the SUV, two comparisons put it in place.

**The same gap exists in gas — bigger.** A 32-MPG gas sedan versus a 19-MPG gas SUV at $3.50/gallon is ~11¢ versus ~18.4¢ per mile — a 7-cent gap, versus the EV pair's ~2.4-cent gap. Choosing the bigger vehicle costs extra in any powertrain; electricity just shrinks the penalty. The electric truck at 8.5¢/mile still undercuts the gas *sedan* at 11¢.

**Off-peak charging can erase the whole difference.** Move that large SUV's charging to a $0.10 [off-peak rate](/blog/how-much-you-save-overnight/) and its cost per mile drops to ~4.5¢ — cheaper than the sedan on the flat rate. The rate lever is bigger than the vehicle-size lever, which is a genuinely useful reframe: the family that needs the SUV loses less by picking it than by neglecting the schedule.

## Second-order costs of the bigger battery

Two adjacent effects of choosing big, worth knowing. **Charging time:** the SUV's lower efficiency plus its typically larger battery means more kWh per fill — the same [40A charger](/blog/how-to-choose-between-32a-40a-48a/) takes proportionally longer, though overnight windows absorb it easily for daily driving. **Winter amplification:** heavy vehicles' efficiency drops in cold like everyone's, but the absolute kWh (and dollars) of the drop are larger — the truck's [winter bill swing](/blog/charging-in-extreme-heat-cost/) is more noticeable than the sedan's. Neither changes the verdict; both belong in an honest picture.

## Choosing with clear eyes

The efficiency spec deserves a place in your comparison shopping — between two similar SUVs, a 0.5 mi/kWh difference is ~$100+/year forever — but it shouldn't outrank what the vehicle is *for*. The family that needs three rows, the trades that need a bed, the towing that needs the truck: the charging premium for those capabilities is tens of dollars a month, less than the same choice costs in gasoline, and partially or fully recoverable through rate optimization. Buy the size your life requires, check the efficiency spec *within* that size class, and put your optimization energy into the [schedule](/blog/how-much-you-save-overnight/) rather than the showroom regret.

## What drives the efficiency gap

Understanding *why* the gap exists helps you predict it for any model you're considering. Three physical factors do nearly all the work. **Weight:** every extra thousand pounds costs energy to accelerate and (partially recovered by regen) to stop — the SUV's mass is a permanent tax, steepest in stop-and-go driving. **Aerodynamics:** frontal area and shape dominate at highway speed, where pushing a tall, wide box through air costs disproportionately more — this is why the sedan/SUV gap widens on road trips and narrows in the city. **Tires:** bigger, wider, more aggressive tires roll with more resistance; even within one model, the trim with 21-inch wheels measurably out-consumes the 18-inch version. The practical use: when comparing specific vehicles, weight and shape tell you which way the efficiency spec will point before you look it up — and the spec confirms the magnitude.

## The seasonal multiplier on size

One more pattern worth knowing: everything that makes a bigger EV cost more per mile scales up in winter. The larger cabin takes more energy to heat, the bigger battery takes more energy to condition, and the same percentage efficiency drop lands on a bigger baseline — so the truck's [winter premium](/blog/charging-in-extreme-heat-cost/) in absolute dollars can be double the sedan's. A household budgeting for a large EV in a cold climate should run the winter version of the cost-per-mile math, not just the annual average: the January bill is where the size choice shows up most vividly, and knowing that in advance turns a surprise into a line item.

## The used-market angle

Efficiency's per-mile cost also shapes used-EV shopping, where the effect compounds with age. An older large EV with a degraded battery pays twice: lower efficiency than modern designs *and* fewer usable kWh per charge — so its cost per mile can approach double a new efficient sedan's. Conversely, used efficient sedans are often the cheapest-per-mile vehicles on the road, period. When evaluating any used EV, ask for (or estimate) its real-world mi/kWh rather than assuming the original spec — a test drive with the trip meter reset tells you in twenty minutes. The [cost-per-mile method](/blog/how-to-estimate-cost-per-mile/) works identically on a used car; the efficiency input is just more individual.

## Quick sanity check before you decide

If the size decision is genuinely open, spend one evening on this: take your real annual miles, compute annual charging cost at each candidate's efficiency, and look at the difference as a share of the vehicles' price gap. In most cases the charging delta ($100–450/year) is small next to the purchase-price difference between classes — meaning the honest decision drivers are needs, purchase price, insurance, and parking, with charging cost a tiebreaker at most. The math takes ten minutes and permanently retires the nagging "but what will it cost to charge?" question from the showroom.

## Quick answers

**Do bigger EVs cost more per kWh to charge?** No — the rate is the rate. They use more kWh per mile; that's the entire difference.

**Does the bigger battery itself cost more to "fill"?** A full charge costs more (more kWh), but you're buying more range — per-mile cost is the fair comparison.

**Do two efficient trips beat one SUV trip?** If the sedan can genuinely do the job, yes — many two-EV households route the long errands to the [efficient car](/blog/two-evs-monthly-charging-cost/) by habit.

One final framing for the showroom: the charging-cost gap between vehicle sizes is the most *predictable* cost difference you'll ever evaluate — it's physics, it's linear with your miles, and it never surprises you later. Price it once with your real numbers, weigh it against what the bigger vehicle does for your life, and decide with clear eyes. Most households conclude the capability is worth the cents; the point is concluding it on purpose.
