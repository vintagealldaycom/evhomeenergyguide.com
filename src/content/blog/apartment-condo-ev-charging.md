The most common reason people delay going electric isn't range or price — it's parking. "I live in an apartment; I can't charge" feels like a hard stop. It isn't. Apartment and condo charging is genuinely harder than a suburban garage, but the paths are more numerous and more realistic than the folk wisdom suggests, and they're multiplying every year. Here's the honest map, ranked from easiest to most ambitious.

## First, reframe the requirement

The garage-owner's mental model — full charge available every night — is more than most drivers actually need. The [average driver covers ~37 miles a day](/): roughly 11–13 kWh, or two to three hours of Level 2 charging *per week's worth* in a couple of sessions. An apartment dweller doesn't need a personal charger; they need **routine access to enough charging** — a different, much more solvable problem. Every path below is a way of assembling that access.

## Path 1: What your building already has (or will approve)

Start closer than you think: many multifamily properties already have some charging, and many more will approve modest requests framed well. The moves, in order of size: **an existing outlet near your spot** — deeded condo spots and assigned apartment spaces sometimes sit near a standard outlet; permission plus a fair electricity arrangement can make [Level 1](/blog/trickle-charging-level-1/) your quiet nightly solution, and at apartment-typical mileage, Level 1 covers more residents than expect it. **A formal request** — the [landlord playbook](/blog/how-to-ask-landlord-about-ev-charging/) (smallest sufficient ask, professional installation, clear electricity plan) applies to property managers too, and larger properties respond to the amenity framing: registered tenant demand is how buildings decide to install shared charging. **Right-to-charge laws** — a growing set of jurisdictions require landlords and HOAs to permit reasonable charging installations at the resident's expense; condo owners in particular should know the [HOA-specific version](/blog/hoa-rules-ev-chargers/) of these rules before assuming no.

The pattern that works is **anchoring**: one or two reliable weekly sessions (workplace being gold) covering the baseline, with errand charging as top-up and fast charging as exception. Residents who try to live entirely on opportunistic fast charging pay [several times the home rate](/blog/how-to-estimate-cost-per-mile/) and feel the friction; residents with an anchor barely think about charging at all. Cost-wise, public Level 2 typically lands between home rates and fast charging — an apartment driver's blended cost usually runs modestly above a homeowner's and still comfortably below gasoline.

## Path 3: Building the infrastructure yourself (condo owners especially)

Owners of deeded parking — condo spots, townhome garages — can often install real charging, with the [HOA process](/blog/hoa-rules-ev-chargers/) as the gatekeeper rather than a landlord. The realistic versions: a **dedicated circuit to your deeded spot** where the building's electrical room is reachable (an electrician quotes the run like any [distance-driven install](/blog/charger-distance-from-panel/), and right-to-charge laws in many places oblige the HOA to permit reasonable versions of it at your cost); a **submetered arrangement** where your charging bills to your unit — the clean answer to "who pays" in shared-meter buildings; or the **community install** — the patient, highest-leverage move where you become the resident who organizes neighbor demand, brings the turnkey multifamily-charging providers and utility make-ready programs to the board, and gets shared Level 2 into the lot for everyone. That last one is slower but increasingly common: utility programs now cover much of the infrastructure cost for multifamily properties precisely because this gap is well understood.

## Making any path livable

Whichever path you assemble, three habits smooth apartment EV life. **Charge to your week, not your day** — with routine access rather than nightly access, think in weekly kWh and keep the battery cruising in the [comfortable middle](/blog/charge-to-100-or-80/) rather than topping daily. **Know your real numbers** — a month of tracked driving usually reveals you need less charging than anxiety predicted, and it right-sizes every request you make of landlords and boards. **Keep the Level 1 cable in the trunk** — visits to houses, hotels with outlets, and workplace loading docks all become opportunistic charging with the [humble included cable](/blog/trickle-charging-level-1/) at hand.

## The math that makes it all less scary

Run the honest weekly numbers once and the whole topic shrinks. A 250-mile week in an efficient EV is roughly 75–85 kWh with [losses](/blog/charging-efficiency-loss/) — about eleven hours of Level 2 total. Two workplace charging days cover it entirely. One workplace day plus a weekly grocery-run session covers it. A nightly Level 1 outlet at your spot covers it twice over. Even the no-anchor worst case — public Level 2 only — is three or four errand-timed sessions, and at typical public rates the *entire month* of driving energy runs $45–70: more than a garage owner pays, far less than the gas it replaces. The apartment charging problem, quantified, is "find eleven weekly hours of parking near a plug" — which is why most residents who run the numbers stop treating it as a dealbreaker and start treating it as a scheduling puzzle with several working answers.

The landscape is also moving in your favor faster than any other part of EV ownership: workplace charging programs, curbside municipal chargers, right-to-charge statutes, and utility multifamily funding all expanded dramatically in the past few years and continue to. The path you assemble today is the hardest version you'll ever have to run — every lease renewal and every year brings more options, which is worth remembering when the current puzzle feels tight.

## Quick answers

**Is apartment EV ownership actually more expensive?** Modestly — a public-Level-2-anchored routine typically costs more per mile than home charging and much less than gas. The premium is the price of the parking situation, not the car.

**Can I run a cord from my window/balcony to the lot?** Almost universally no — building rules, trip hazards, and weather exposure make improvised cords the fastest way to lose charging privileges entirely. Ask for the outlet instead.

**Do buildings ever charge residents extra for EV-outlet electricity?** Fair-reimbursement arrangements (metered or estimated) are normal; the [transparent math](/blog/how-to-estimate-cost-per-mile/) keeps them honest — typical apartment mileage is $20–40/month of electricity.

**Should parking situation change which EV I buy?** It nudges: efficient models stretch each session further, and [fast-charging capability](/blog/ac-vs-dc-charging/) matters more when it's your backstop. But any modern EV works on these paths.

## The bottom line

Apartment and condo charging is a logistics problem with multiple working answers, not a disqualification: an outlet with permission, a workplace anchor, an errand-woven public routine, a deeded-spot install, or the community project that fixes the building for good. Most residents assemble coverage from two of these and stop thinking about it. The garage is a convenience, not a prerequisite — what you need is a routine, and routines are buildable.
