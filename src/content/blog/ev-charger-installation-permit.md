It's the question everyone hopes has a "no" answer: do you really need a permit to put a charger in your own garage? For a Level 2 install, the honest answer is almost always **yes** — and while it's tempting to skip the paperwork, doing so creates risks that dwarf the modest permit fee. Here's what permits are for, when you need one, and why the process is worth it even when it feels like bureaucracy.

## The short answer

**Installing a Level 2 charger (240V) almost always requires an electrical permit.** You're adding a significant new circuit to your home's electrical system, and virtually every jurisdiction treats that as permit-worthy electrical work — the same category as adding a subpanel or wiring a hot tub.

**Simply plugging a Level 1 charger into an existing standard outlet does not** — you're not modifying anything, just using an outlet as intended. The permit question is really about the *electrical work* (new circuit, new outlet, panel changes), not about the charger itself.

Rules vary by city, county, and state, so the definitive answer always comes from your local building department — but the default assumption for any 240V work should be "permit required."

It's easy to see a permit as a fee-collection ritual, but the requirement exists for reasons that directly protect you:

That insurance point deserves emphasis. If an unpermitted charging circuit ever causes a fire, your insurer can deny the claim on the grounds that the work was unpermitted and uninspected — turning a covered accident into a total loss. The permit fee is trivial insurance against that scenario.

## Who handles the permit?

Good news: if you hire a licensed electrician (which you should — a dedicated EV circuit is [not a DIY job](/blog/dedicated-circuit-ev-charger/)), **they typically pull the permit for you** as part of the job. It's a routine step for them, and reputable electricians won't do the work without it. You often won't touch the paperwork at all.

## Rebates often *require* it

Here's a reason to want the permit: many utility and government EV-charger rebates and tax incentives **require proof of a permitted, inspected installation** to qualify. Skipping the permit to save time can cost you hundreds in rebates you'd otherwise collect. Check your utility's EV program — the permitted install may pay for itself.

<div class="callout warn"><span class="co-icon">⚠</span><div><strong>The "it's just a dryer outlet" trap:</strong> reusing or adding a 240V outlet still typically requires a permit, and an old dryer circuit often <em>isn't</em> rated for continuous EV load anyway. "It already has an outlet" doesn't make it safe or legal for charging — verify with an electrician.</div></div>

## What it costs and how long it takes

Permit fees are usually modest — often in the tens to low hundreds of dollars, folded into your electrician's quote. The inspection adds a little time (scheduling around the inspector's availability), but rarely more than a few days to a couple of weeks depending on your jurisdiction's backlog. In the context of a charger installation that itself runs several hundred to a couple thousand dollars, the permit is a small line item with outsized protective value.

## The bottom line

For Level 2 home charging: get the permit. It's almost certainly required, your electrician handles it, it protects your insurance and your home's resale value, it often unlocks rebates, and — most importantly — the inspection it triggers is an expert verifying that a high-power circuit in your home won't burn it down. The only thing skipping it saves is a small fee and a few days, at the cost of real financial and safety exposure. It's one of the easiest good decisions in the whole EV setup process.

## What the inspector actually checks

Demystifying the inspection removes most of the dread around it. The inspector isn't looking to fail you or find fault with your home — they're verifying a short list of safety essentials: that the wire gauge matches the breaker size, that the breaker is correctly rated for the load, that terminations are properly made and torqued, that grounding is correct, that GFCI protection is present where required, and that the whole install matches the permit and code. For a competent licensed electrician, passing is routine — this is bread-and-butter work they do to code by habit. The inspection is really a safety net that catches the rare mistake, not a hurdle designed to trip up honest work. When your electrician pulls the permit and does the job right, the inspection is a brief formality that ends with a sign-off and peace of mind.

## The resale reality

The permit's resale value is easy to underestimate until you're selling. Home inspections and disclosure requirements increasingly surface EV charging installations, and an unpermitted 240V circuit is exactly the kind of finding that makes buyers nervous, triggers renegotiation, or forces you to retroactively permit and inspect work that's now buried behind finished walls — sometimes at real expense and hassle. A permitted, inspected install, by contrast, is a clean line item that a growing number of EV-shopping buyers actively want to see. As EVs become mainstream, "permitted Level 2 charging installed" is shifting from a neutral detail to a selling point. Spending a small fee and a few days now to have proper documentation is not just a safety and insurance decision — it's protecting the transferable value of an improvement you're making to your home.

## Quick answers

**How much does a permit cost?** Typically tens to low hundreds of dollars, folded into the electrician's quote — small next to the install itself.

**Will an inspector come into my house?** Usually just to the panel and the install location, briefly — it's a focused electrical inspection, not a home audit.

**What if previous owners installed a charger without a permit?** Consider having an electrician inspect it — retroactive permitting varies by jurisdiction, but a safety check is wise regardless.
