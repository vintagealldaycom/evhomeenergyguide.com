"What does it actually cost you per mile?" is the question every EV owner gets at a barbecue — and the one calculation genuinely worth knowing cold, because it's the number that captures the entire financial case for driving electric. The good news: it takes three numbers and one division, and once you've done it with your own figures, you'll never fumble the barbecue question again.

## The formula

Your cost per mile is simply what you pay for energy divided by how far that energy takes you:

That's it: **rate ÷ miles-per-kWh = cost per mile.** A car getting 3.5 mi/kWh on $0.16 electricity costs under a nickel a mile to drive.

## Making it honest: the efficiency-loss adjustment

One refinement makes your number bill-accurate. Your car's efficiency screen measures energy *from the battery*, but you're billed for energy *from the wall* — and [charging loses about 10–15%](/blog/charging-efficiency-loss/) between the two. To get the true billed cost, add that overhead: multiply your result by about 1.12, or equivalently use your charger's wall-side kWh in the math. Our 4.6-cent example becomes a true ~5.1 cents per mile. Small difference, but it's the honest number — and it's still spectacular next to gasoline.

## The gas comparison

To translate for the barbecue: a 30-MPG gas car at $3.50/gallon costs about 11.7 cents per mile — more than double our example EV. Put another way, our EV's 5.1 cents per mile is the gas-car equivalent of paying about $1.50 a gallon. The comparison scales with your local prices, so run it with your own numbers:

## What moves your number

Your cost per mile isn't fixed — four levers move it, two of which you control:

**Your rate (you control it somewhat).** Charging during [off-peak hours](/blog/how-much-you-save-overnight/) on a time-of-use plan is the single biggest lever — dropping from a $0.20 peak rate to a $0.10 overnight rate literally halves your cost per mile for a one-time scheduling setup.

**Your efficiency (you influence it).** Gentle driving, moderate highway speeds, and mild weather raise miles per kWh; winter cold, high speed, and heavy loads lower it. Expect your real-world number to swing seasonally — winter cost per mile can run meaningfully higher than summer's, which is normal.

**Where you charge (you choose it).** These numbers are *home charging* numbers. DC fast charging typically costs 3–4× more per kWh, pushing cost per mile toward or past gas parity — fine for road trips, expensive as a habit. Home is where the savings live.

**Your car (fixed once bought).** A big heavy EV at 2.5 mi/kWh costs 40% more per mile than an efficient one at 3.5 on the same rate — worth knowing when comparing models.

## The number in context

A last calibration: cost per mile is powerful precisely because it normalizes everything. Rates differ by state (from under $0.10 to over $0.30 per kWh), cars differ in efficiency, and gas prices swing — but cost per mile absorbs all of it into one comparable figure. It's how you can fairly weigh your EV against your old car, your neighbor's hybrid, or a prospective new EV with different efficiency. It also exposes the traps: a cheap-to-buy EV with poor efficiency in a high-rate state can cost more per mile than an efficient one somewhere cheap — window stickers won't tell you that, but two divisions will. Keep your personal number current (summer and winter versions), and every fuel-cost conversation, purchase comparison, and "is this trip cheaper to drive or fly" question becomes thirty seconds of arithmetic instead of a guess.

## Track it once, know it forever

The best version of this calculation uses a full month of your own data: your charger's total wall-side kWh for the month, your bill's rate, and your car's miles driven. Divide total cost by total miles and you have your true, personal, loss-inclusive cost per mile — no estimates, no asterisks. Do it once in summer and once in winter and you'll know your range of reality better than any online calculator can guess it. Then automate the savings: set your [off-peak schedule](/blog/how-much-you-save-overnight/), let the cheap hours do the work, and enjoy having the most satisfying answer at the barbecue.

## Quick answers

**Should I use the EPA efficiency or my own?** Yours — the trip screen's long-term average reflects your climate, speeds, and habits. The sticker is a stranger's number.

**Does charging speed change cost per mile?** No — you pay per [kWh, not kW](/blog/kw-vs-kwh-explained/). Timing changes cost; speed doesn't.

**How often should I re-run the math?** Twice: once in summer, once in winter. Those two numbers bracket your reality; everything else falls between.

## The two-EV and road-trip variations

The method extends naturally. For a **two-EV household**, run it per car — different efficiencies mean different costs per mile, and the gap is often surprising: a compact EV at 3.8 mi/kWh and a large SUV at 2.4 mi/kWh on the same $0.16 rate cost ~4.7 and ~7.5 cents per mile respectively. Knowing both numbers helps you choose which car takes the long errand. For **road trips**, redo the math with fast-charging prices: at $0.45/kWh, that same 3.5 mi/kWh car costs ~14.4 cents per mile — suddenly *above* the 30-MPG gas car. That flip is worth internalizing: EVs win decisively on home charging and merely tie-or-lose on fast charging, which is why the economics reward home-charging-heavy usage and why frequent fast-charger reliance erodes the savings story. Your blended real-world cost sits between the two, weighted by how you actually charge.

## Beyond electricity: the per-mile picture

Cost per mile of *energy* is the headline, but if you're comparing total ownership against a gas car, two adjacent facts strengthen the EV case further: maintenance per mile runs lower (no oil changes, minimal brake wear thanks to regen), while tires can run slightly higher (EV weight and torque). Insurance and depreciation vary too much by model to generalize. For the barbecue conversation, energy cost per mile is the honest, easily-verified core — but when someone extends the debate to total costs, the maintenance advantage is the legitimate second act. What you shouldn't do is pad the energy number itself: the clean rate-divided-by-efficiency figure, loss-adjusted, is defensible to any skeptic precisely because it comes straight off your bill and your trip screen.
