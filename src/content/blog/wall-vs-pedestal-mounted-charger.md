Every home charger needs to live somewhere, and for most garages the answer is obvious: on the wall, near the panel, done. But a meaningful minority of homes — driveway parkers, detached-pad parkers, homes where the wall is in the wrong place — face a genuine choice between wall mounting and a pedestal (a freestanding post the charger mounts to). The decision affects cost, convenience, durability, and even resale, so here's the honest comparison.

## The default case for the wall

Wall mounting wins on simplicity and cost wherever a suitable wall exists near the parking spot. The charger bolts to the structure you already own, the wiring runs through or along that structure, and the installation is the straightforward version every electrician has done dozens of times. There's no concrete work, no post to set, no trenching beyond what the wire run itself requires. The charger sits at a comfortable height, the [cable management](/blog/charger-cable-length-driveway/) hangs naturally, and the whole assembly is sheltered by whatever shelters the wall — which, in a garage, is the entire building.

If your car parks in a garage or against a house wall, the wall wins and this article is short. The interesting decision belongs to everyone else.

## When the pedestal earns its cost

A pedestal exists to solve one problem: **the charger needs to be where no wall is.** That situation is more common than it sounds. The driveway parking spot sits ten feet from the house. The parking pad is beside a detached garage whose near wall faces the wrong way. The home is on a lot where cars park along a fence line or in a side yard. In each case the wall-mount alternative means a long cable stretched across a walkway — a [tripping hazard and a cable-wear problem](/blog/charger-cable-length-driveway/) — or a charger mounted so far from the car that daily use becomes an errand.

The pedestal deletes the compromise. A post set at the parking spot puts the connector within arm's reach of the charge port, the cable stays short and off the ground, and the daily experience matches a garage install. Households with [two cars on a shared pad](/blog/charger-placement-two-car-garage/) often find a single well-placed pedestal between the spots serves both better than any wall position could.

What you pay for that placement: the pedestal hardware itself (often a few hundred dollars, sometimes charger-brand-specific), a concrete footing or base, and — usually the biggest line — **trenching** to run the circuit underground from the house to the post. Trenching cost scales with distance and what's between here and there (lawn is cheap, established hardscape is not), and the run's length also factors into [wire sizing](/blog/charger-distance-from-panel/). Get the full path priced before falling in love with the placement.

## The weather dimension

A wall-mounted charger in a garage lives an easy life. A pedestal charger lives outdoors in everything your climate offers, which raises three requirements a garage install can ignore. The charger itself needs a proper [NEMA weather rating](/blog/charger-nema-rating-weather/) — most quality units are rated for outdoor use, but it becomes a hard requirement rather than a nice-to-have. The installation details need [weatherproofing done right](/blog/weatherproofing-outdoor-ev-charger/): sealed conduit entries, weather-rated fittings, drainage-conscious mounting. And the connector needs a holster position that sheds water rather than collecting it — the humble detail that prevents most outdoor [nuisance ground-faults](/blog/charger-says-ground-fault/). None of this is exotic; all of it is mandatory, and it's covered in depth in the [indoor vs outdoor installation guide](/blog/indoor-vs-outdoor-charger-install/).

## Durability, security, and the long run

Pedestals take physical life differently than walls. On the plus side, a well-set post is immune to the garage wall's classic hazard — the car itself, which bumps, scrapes, and occasionally backs into wall-mounted equipment. On the minus side, the pedestal stands in the open: snowplows and lawn equipment need to know it's there (a bollard or reflective marker is cheap protection), and an exposed charger in some neighborhoods invites curiosity that a garage unit never faces — worth considering a unit with access control (app-lock or RFID) if the pedestal fronts a shared or visible space. Cable management also matters more outdoors: a retractable or well-holstered cable stays off wet, freezing, abrasive ground, while a cable habitually dropped on a driveway ages in dog years.

## Making the call

The decision usually makes itself once you stand where the car actually parks and look around. A suitable wall within a short cable's reach: wall mount, spend the savings on a [longer-warranty unit](/blog/what-makes-a-charger-smart/). No wall in reach, or a cable that would cross where people walk: pedestal, and price the trench honestly. Genuinely borderline cases — a wall *almost* close enough — often resolve toward the wall with a longer-cable charger model, which is cheaper than concrete and trenching by a comfortable margin. And if a future driveway reconfiguration or garage build is plausible, remember the pedestal is semi-permanent: the post moves only with more concrete work, while a wall unit relocates with an electrician's visit.

## The hybrid and edge cases

A few real-world layouts don't fit the binary cleanly, and they have known answers. **The garage-adjacent driveway spot** — car outside, garage wall right there: an outdoor-rated wall unit on the garage's exterior face serves the driveway at wall-mount cost, no pedestal needed; this is the most common "pedestal question" that isn't one. **The shared driveway or duplex pad** — a pedestal between two households' spots raises access and billing questions a wall unit on one party's structure doesn't; units with [RFID or app authorization](/blog/what-makes-a-charger-smart/) solve the sharing cleanly. **The future-garage lot** — building a garage in a few years? A pedestal placed with the future structure in mind (or a deliberately temporary wall solution) beats concrete you'll demolish. In every edge case, the same two questions decide: where do the cars actually park, and what does the cable have to reach? Answer those honestly and the mounting choice mostly makes itself.

One last practical note: whichever mounting you choose, photograph the finished install — unit, mounting, and (for pedestals) the trench route before backfill. The photos serve rebate applications, future electricians, resale documentation, and the inevitable someday question of "what exactly runs under that flowerbed." Thirty seconds of documentation on install day is the cheapest part of the whole project.

## Quick answers

**Do pedestals work with any charger?** Many are brand-specific mounts; universal pedestals exist. Confirm compatibility before ordering either piece.

**Can I DIY the pedestal footing?** The concrete, perhaps — the circuit, [no](/blog/install-home-charger-yourself/). Underground electrical runs have their own code requirements (burial depth, conduit type) squarely in electrician territory.

**Is a pedestal worth it for resale?** A well-placed outdoor charging point is an increasingly marketable feature, particularly where garage-less homes are common. Permitted and documented, it's a genuine plus.

**What about a short post on a garage exterior wall instead?** If the wall is close but the mounting height or surface is wrong, wall-standoff brackets solve it far cheaper than a full pedestal — ask your installer.
