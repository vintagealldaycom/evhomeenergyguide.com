Renting with an EV means the most important charging conversation isn't with an electrician — it's with your landlord. The good news: this ask succeeds far more often than renters expect, especially when it's framed well, scoped modestly, and backed by the growing legal and market tailwinds behind tenant charging. Here's the playbook.

## Know what you're actually asking for

The strongest move is asking for the *smallest sufficient thing* — and for most renters, that's not a fancy charger install. Work up this ladder:

The middle rung is the renter's sweet spot: the landlord gets a permanent property improvement, you get real Level 2 charging, and your charger investment [moves with you](/blog/portable-vs-wall-mounted-charger/).

## Frame the pitch in the landlord's interest

Landlords say yes to things that protect and improve their property. Build the pitch on their priorities, not your convenience:

    <div class="vz-tile"><span class="vz-ico ok">🏠</span><strong>Property value</strong><span>EV-ready parking is an increasingly marketable amenity — the outlet outlasts your tenancy and attracts future tenants.</span></div>

Put it in writing — a short, friendly proposal covering the what (one 240V outlet at your parking spot), the who-pays (your offer), the electricity plan, and the professional/permitted installation. A one-page written proposal signals seriousness and gives the landlord something easy to say yes to.

## Handling the electricity question

"Who pays for the power?" is the landlord's most common concern, and coming prepared with options defuses it. If your parking is on your own meter, it's a non-issue — you're just using your own electricity. If it's on house power, offer solutions: a smart charger or plug-in energy meter that logs your exact kWh for reimbursement at the utility rate, a flat monthly adder to rent based on [honest math](/blog/how-to-estimate-cost-per-mile/) (an average commuter uses roughly $30–60/month of charging electricity — show the calculation), or a dedicated sub-meter if the property wants precision. Landlords mostly fear open-ended costs; a concrete, auditable plan turns the objection into a detail.

## Know your local tailwinds

Before the conversation, spend ten minutes checking two things. First, **right-to-charge laws**: a growing number of states and cities require landlords or HOAs to permit reasonable EV charging installations (typically at the tenant's expense, with conditions) — if you're in one, your ask has legal backing, though leading with cooperation beats leading with statute. Second, **incentives**: many utilities offer rebates for charger or outlet installation that apply to rental properties, sometimes generously — bringing "and there's a rebate that covers part of it" to the conversation makes yes easier. Your utility's EV page and a search for your state plus "right to charge" cover both.

## If the answer is no

A no isn't the end of charging — it's a routing instruction. Your options, roughly in order: **Level 1 on any outlet you do have access to** (with permission — even a shared garage outlet at a fair reimbursement often covers a [low-mileage driver](/blog/trickle-charging-level-1/) entirely); **workplace charging** if your employer offers it; **public Level 2 near home** worked into your routine (gym, grocery, transit lot); and **DC fast charging** as the pricier backstop. Many renters run happily for years on a workplace-plus-public blend. And when your lease renews or you move, "EV charging possible?" moves up your apartment-hunting checklist — a question more listings can answer positively every year.

## Quick answers

**Who owns the outlet when I move?** The property — that's part of the pitch. Your portable charger, and its value, leave with you.

**What's a fair flat electricity adder?** Your monthly kWh estimate × the utility rate, shown transparently — the [cost-per-mile method](/blog/how-to-estimate-cost-per-mile/) gives the kWh. Offer to true-up if usage changes.

**Can a landlord charge me a premium on the electricity?** Rules vary by jurisdiction on reselling power — metered reimbursement at the utility rate is the clean, uncontroversial arrangement.

## The property-manager variation

If your "landlord" is a management company or a large apartment complex, the playbook shifts slightly. Individual outlets at assigned parking may be impractical, but complexes increasingly respond to a different pitch: shared EV charging as an amenity. Here your one-page proposal becomes a nudge — pointing the manager toward the turnkey providers that install and operate shared chargers for multifamily properties (often with utility make-ready programs covering much of the infrastructure cost), and registering formal interest so demand is documented. You may not get a personal outlet, but you can be the tenant whose request tips the property into installing shared Level 2 in the lot — a slower win that benefits everyone. Meanwhile, the fallback ladder (workplace, public Level 2, routine-integrated charging) carries you. In right-to-charge jurisdictions, larger properties often have explicit obligations for reasonable accommodation at assigned spaces — worth knowing before the conversation, even if you never need to cite it.

## What to put in the one-pager

Since the written proposal does the heavy lifting, here's its skeleton. One paragraph on the request: a licensed electrician installs one 240V outlet at your parking space, permitted and inspected, at your cost (or your proposed split). One paragraph on electricity: your metering or flat-adder plan, with the monthly estimate shown. One paragraph on their upside: a permanent, code-compliant improvement that markets to future tenants, with any utility rebate you found named and linked. Close with logistics: you'll coordinate everything; they sign one approval. Attach nothing else — brevity signals that this is as simple as it actually is. If your jurisdiction has a right-to-charge law, keep it out of the letter and in your back pocket; leading with cooperation gets faster yeses, and the statute loses nothing by waiting quietly in reserve.

## Timing and tone

Two soft factors move these conversations more than renters expect. **Timing:** raise it at lease renewal, when your value as a staying tenant is on the table, or when the property is upgrading anything electrical anyway (the marginal cost of adding an outlet during other work is small). **Tone:** you're proposing an improvement, not requesting a favor — landlords hear complaints all day, and a tenant who arrives with a researched, costed, professionally-scoped proposal that makes their property better is a pleasant anomaly. Keep the first conversation short, leave the one-pager, and offer to answer questions after they've sat with it. Most nos are really "this sounds like hassle" — and everything in this playbook is designed to make it sound (and be) the opposite.
