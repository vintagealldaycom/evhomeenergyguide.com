On paper it's the same project: a charger, a circuit, a wall. In practice, an indoor garage installation and an outdoor one are meaningfully different jobs — different equipment requirements, different code details, different long-term maintenance realities. Neither is bad; outdoor installs are routine and reliable when done to their own standard. The mistakes happen when an outdoor install is treated as an indoor one with weather. Here's what actually changes when the charger steps outside.

## The equipment requirement: ratings stop being optional

Indoors, a charger's weather rating is trivia — the garage is the enclosure. Outdoors, the rating becomes the foundation of the whole install: the unit needs a [NEMA enclosure rating appropriate for exposed weather](/blog/charger-nema-rating-weather/) (NEMA 3R at minimum for rain-exposed placements; NEMA 4 for driven-rain and washdown exposure), and every component in the chain — the receptacle if [plug-in](/blog/nema-14-50-vs-hardwired-charger/), the fittings, the conduit — needs its own weather-rated version. Most quality home chargers ship outdoor-rated, so this is usually a verification rather than a constraint — but it's a hard verification, because an indoor-only unit outdoors isn't a budget choice, it's a countdown.

## The code differences: GFCI and wet-location rules

Two code threads tighten outdoors. **GFCI:** outdoor receptacles require GFCI protection essentially without exception, and outdoor circuits generally live under stricter ground-fault rules — which occasionally produces the [double-protection nuisance-trip dance](/blog/nuisance-tripping-vs-real-fault/) between a GFCI breaker and the charger's own built-in detection. It's a known, solvable interaction (equipment choice and configuration matter), and it's a reason [hardwired outdoor installs](/blog/nema-14-50-vs-hardwired-charger/) — where the charger's internal protection can stand alone under many codes — are often the smoother outdoor path. **Wet-location wiring:** conductors, boxes, and fittings on the exposed portion must carry wet-location ratings, and every penetration into the building envelope gets properly sealed — details your electrician handles as routine, but which explain why an outdoor quote runs modestly higher than the same-distance indoor one.

## The placement details that earn their keep

Outdoor placement adds a layer of small decisions that indoor installs never face — each minor, together decisive for how the install ages. **Orientation and shelter:** under an eave or on a north-or-shaded face beats broiling on a south wall — [heat is the enemy of electronics](/blog/charging-in-extreme-heat-cost/), and afternoon sun on a dark enclosure adds real thermal stress every summer day. **Drainage logic:** mount so water sheds off and away — never where a roofline concentrates runoff onto the unit, never in a spot that splashes from below. **The holster question:** the connector's resting position should point down or seal against weather so the [business end never collects standing water](/blog/charging-ev-in-rain/) — the single most effective habit-and-hardware combination against outdoor nuisance ground-faults. **Cable environment:** outdoors, the [cable's life](/blog/charger-cable-length-driveway/) includes UV, freeze-thaw, and grit; a proper hook that keeps the full coil off the ground goes from tidy to essential. The [weatherproofing guide](/blog/weatherproofing-outdoor-ev-charger/) turns all of this into a checklist.

## Living with each, long-term

Daily use is nearly identical — [charging in rain and snow is safe by design](/blog/charging-ev-in-rain/) either way — but the maintenance rhythm differs. The garage unit's life is uneventful: the [monthly warmth check](/blog/charger-plug-or-outlet-feels-hot/), an occasional dusting, done. The outdoor unit earns a seasonal minute more: a spring look at seals, gaskets, and the in-use cover's spring; a summer awareness of [thermal derating](/blog/charging-speed-suddenly-dropped/) on brutal days; a winter habit of knocking snow and ice off the holster before plugging in. Outdoor units also age visibly — sun-faded enclosures, stiffer cables — well before they age functionally; a quality outdoor-rated unit under an eave routinely runs a decade-plus. And one asymmetry favors outdoors: no garage means no garage-door dependency — household members, guests, and the [second car in the driveway](/blog/charger-placement-two-car-garage/) all reach an outdoor unit freely, which some households come to prefer.

## Choosing, when you have the choice

If both placements are genuinely available, the tiebreakers: the garage wins on equipment longevity, simpler code path, and security; outdoors wins on universal access and on serving cars that actually park outside — and [the car's real parking spot should dominate](/blog/charger-cable-length-driveway/), because a garage charger serving a driveway car through a propped door is the worst of both worlds. Cost differences are usually modest (rated components and sealing details, not a different magnitude of project), so let the cars' actual habits decide, not the equipment's preferences.

## The cost picture, honestly itemized

Since "how much more is outdoor?" is the natural next question: the delta is real but modest for typical installs. The rated receptacle and in-use cover (plug-in installs), weather-rated fittings and sealant work, and any GFCI-breaker requirement together typically add a minor-to-moderate premium over the identical-distance indoor job — the kind of difference measured in low hundreds, not multiples. The exceptions that grow it: [pedestal mounting](/blog/wall-vs-pedestal-mounted-charger/) (concrete and trenching are their own project), long exposed conduit runs, and retrofit sealing on difficult wall types. What the premium buys is the difference between an install engineered for its environment and one that will spend years fighting it — which makes it the least skippable line on the quote. Get the outdoor details itemized so you can see you're paying for ratings and sealing, not mystery.

## The decision in thirty seconds

Compressed to its essentials: put the charger where the car actually parks — that rule outranks every equipment preference. If that's a garage, install normally and enjoy the easy life. If that's outdoors, verify the NEMA rating, let your electrician handle the GFCI and wet-location details, place for shade and drainage, and adopt the holster habit. The outdoor standard is a short list, fully solved, and modestly priced — the only genuinely bad outcome in this decision is the compromise install that serves the car's real parking spot badly to give the equipment an easier home it didn't need.

## Quick answers

**Can I install an indoor-rated charger under a carport or eave?** Sheltered isn't rated — carports count as damp-to-wet locations in most interpretations. Use an outdoor-rated unit; nearly all quality ones are.

**Does outdoor installation void warranties?** The opposite concern: *improper* outdoor use of indoor-only equipment does. An outdoor-rated unit installed to its rating is fully covered.

**Is theft of outdoor chargers a real issue?** Uncommon but real in some areas — units with locking holsters, app-lock, or RFID authorization address it, and mounting height plus visibility does the rest.

**Do outdoor units charge slower in winter?** The unit doesn't — the [cold battery accepts less](/blog/charge-rate-tapering/) regardless of where the charger hangs. The outdoor difference is your gloves, not your kilowatts.

## The bottom line

Outdoor charger installation is a solved, routine practice with its own standard: rated equipment throughout, GFCI and wet-location code details handled by your electrician, and placement that respects sun, drainage, and the holster. Meet the outdoor standard and the driveway charger serves as long and as reliably as the garage one — the differences are in the details of the install, not in the dependability of the result.
